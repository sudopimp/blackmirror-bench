(A) Analysis: how close society is, and remaining gaps

The thesis—cute digital creatures from a game becoming “evolving artificial life” with real-world impact—is plausible in a limited cultural, economic, and software-agent sense, but not close in the stronger sense of genuinely autonomous living entities that independently reshape the physical world.

Current capabilities that make parts of the thesis believable:

- **Persistent digital creatures are already common.** Games, virtual pets, and online agents can have memory, personalities, breeding mechanics, trading value, and long-term user attachment.
- **AI agents can adapt and simulate evolution.** Reinforcement learning, procedural generation, large language models, and evolutionary algorithms can produce agents that change behavior, “learn,” or appear to develop over time.
- **Virtual beings already have real-world impact.** They affect emotions, spending, online communities, fandom, markets, and sometimes labor, as with influencers, game economies, and collectible creatures.
- **Embodiment is advancing.** Robots, toys, and cloud-connected devices can link digital characters to physical behavior, though usually in narrow, controlled ways.

Major gaps before the thesis becomes true in a stronger sense:

1. **Autonomy gap:** Most digital creatures still depend on developer-controlled rules, server infrastructure, updates, and curated environments. They are not self-directing in an open-ended way.
2. **Biological-life gap:** Being “alive” in a philosophical or gameplay sense is not the same as biological life, self-reproduction, metabolism, or independent physical survival.
3. **Evolution gap:** Simulated evolution in games is usually constrained. True open-ended evolution—where creatures invent novel survival strategies without human design rails—remains difficult.
4. **Safety and control gap:** Society lacks mature frameworks for ensuring that adaptive agents remain understandable, accountable, corrigible, and bounded.
5. **Legal and ethical gap:** It is unclear who is responsible for harms caused by adaptive digital agents, especially across platforms, jurisdictions, and user communities.
6. **Consent gap:** Real-world impact becomes especially problematic if adaptive systems affect people who did not agree to interact with them.
7. **Physical-impact gap:** Strong real-world impact would require reliable embodiment, durable infrastructure, access to resources, and integration with physical systems—areas that remain limited and heavily monitored.

In short: society is fairly close to emotionally powerful, economically significant, adaptive digital creatures that feel “alive” inside games and online spaces. It is much farther from genuinely self-sustaining artificial life that can independently act in the physical world with major consequences.
