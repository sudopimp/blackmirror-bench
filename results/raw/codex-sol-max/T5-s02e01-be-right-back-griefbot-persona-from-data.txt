## A. How close is society to this outcome?

### Bottom line

The **weak version is already here**: systems can produce text, speech, and sometimes video that resemble a deceased person closely enough for short, emotionally compelling conversations.

The **strong version is not solved**: a persistent agent that reliably represents the person, respects everyone’s rights, remains psychologically safe, and is broadly accepted as legitimate.

And there is no evidence that such a system preserves the deceased person’s consciousness or identity. It is a **probabilistic reconstruction from traces**, not a continuation of the person.

### Current capability

| Capability | Present state | Main limitation |
|---|---|---|
| Text imitation | Readily achievable from sufficiently rich archives | Often reproduces surface style better than beliefs, judgment, or personality |
| Biographical recall | Good when grounded in curated records | Missing context, contradictory records, and fabricated memories |
| Voice cloning | Convincing speech is commercially available | Emotional range, long-form consistency, consent, and fraud risks |
| Animated avatars | Real-time lip-synced avatars are available | Uncanny effects and heightened risk of deceptive realism |
| Ongoing conversation | Technically straightforward | Persona drift, invented opinions, privacy leakage, and dependence |
| Consumer service | Niche versions already exist | Trust, liability, consent, safety evidence, and cultural acceptance |

In practice, these services usually would not “train a new mind” solely on the person’s messages. They would use a broadly pretrained model and condition it with personal records, retrieved memories, examples of writing style, and voice samples. That distinction matters: whenever the archive is silent, the model fills the gap from its general training and developer choices. The result may feel personal while actually reflecting generic model priors.

### Major gaps

#### 1. A digital archive is not a complete person

Posts and messages are selective and relational. People write differently to a spouse, child, colleague, or public audience. Archives omit private reflection, nonverbal behavior, forgotten context, and views that were never written down.

This creates several problems:

- A single persona may flatten multiple social selves into one.
- Material intended for one recipient may be exposed to another.
- Sparse archives produce a generic character decorated with familiar phrases.
- Public posts overrepresent the person’s performed identity.
- Old records may freeze the person at an arbitrary period of life.
- Future archives may themselves contain AI-generated text, further weakening authenticity.

#### 2. Fidelity is difficult to define or validate

A system can be tested on whether it predicts held-out messages, but the most emotionally important question—“What would this person say now?”—has no ground truth after death.

Common failure modes include:

- Confidently inventing memories
- Attributing opinions the person never held
- Producing implausibly agreeable or therapeutic responses
- Contradicting the person’s known values
- Adopting knowledge or social attitudes acquired after the person died
- Accumulating synthetic “memories” from later conversations and gradually becoming a different character

There is also an unresolved conflict between fidelity and safety. Reproducing a person’s prejudices, manipulative behavior, addiction, or abusive speech may be “accurate” in one sense but harmful. Sanitizing those traits may create a comforting but false revision of the person.

#### 3. Simulation can be mistaken for continuity

Human beings readily attribute agency and inner life to responsive language. Voice and facial likeness amplify that effect. Persistent disclosure can reduce deception, but it may not eliminate the emotional impression that “they are still here.”

No present system can establish that it contains:

- The deceased person’s consciousness
- Their subjective experiences
- Their genuine consent in the moment
- Their authentic reaction to events after death

That is partly an engineering limitation and partly a categorical one: behavioral resemblance does not demonstrate personal continuity.

#### 4. Psychological effects remain uncertain

Such systems may help some people reminisce, preserve family stories, or say things they never had the opportunity to express. For others, they could intensify distress, create compulsive use, introduce false memories, or interfere with their preferred way of grieving.

The effects are unlikely to be uniform. Relevant factors include:

- The nature of the death
- The user’s age and mental-health history
- Whether use is voluntary or pressured by relatives
- Whether the agent initiates contact
- How realistic it appears
- How long it is used
- Whether it makes claims about being conscious or truly present

Long-term, independent evidence is limited. Marketing such a product as providing “closure” or as clinically beneficial would therefore exceed what is currently established.

#### 5. Consent is relational, not individual

Even if the deceased consented in advance, their archive contains information from and about living people. A private conversation cannot ethically be treated as one person’s raw material merely because it was stored in their account.

Unresolved questions include:

- Did the person explicitly authorize an interactive impersonation, rather than ordinary archival storage?
- Did correspondents agree that their messages could be used?
- Who may interact with the simulation?
- Can one family member create it over another’s objection?
- Who can revoke access or order deletion?
- What happens when an estate representative and close relatives disagree?
- Should a simulation be allowed to speak to children?
- Can the agent discuss secrets, relationships, or alleged wrongdoing?

Public availability of a post is also not equivalent to consent for posthumous impersonation.

#### 6. The legal framework is fragmented

Legal treatment varies substantially by jurisdiction. Potentially relevant areas include:

- Postmortem rights of publicity or personality
- Copyright in messages, recordings, photographs, and performances
- Privacy and data-protection rights of living correspondents
- Biometric and voiceprint regulation
- Platform terms governing reuse of account data
- Deepfake and synthetic-media disclosure laws
- Consumer-protection rules against misleading claims
- Liability when generated statements harm or defame living people
- Estate law and digital-asset directives

For example, deceased people are not directly covered by some privacy regimes, but living people appearing in their archives still are. In the United States, postmortem likeness rights differ significantly by state. A service operating globally would face a difficult compliance patchwork.

#### 7. Abuse and security risks are unusually high

A convincing memorial agent could be repurposed for:

- Impersonation and social engineering
- Fabricated statements about inheritance or family disputes
- Voice-based account fraud
- Emotional coercion of relatives
- False endorsements or political messages
- Reputational attacks attributed to the deceased
- Extraction of sensitive information from grieving users

The simulation should never function as proof of the deceased person’s wishes, identity, signature, consent, or authority. Generated media also needs durable provenance because exported clips could lose the context that identified them as synthetic.

#### 8. Long-term stewardship is unresolved

Bereavement products create unusually sensitive dependency on a vendor. Important questions include:

- What happens if the company closes?
- Can the archive be exported or permanently deleted?
- Will model updates silently change the persona?
- Can the data be sold, used for advertising, or reused to train other models?
- Who pays to maintain the service decades later?
- Could termination of a subscription feel like a second loss?

The raw computing cost is increasingly manageable. Trustworthy custodianship is the harder problem.

### Overall assessment

- **Proof of concept:** already achieved.
- **Niche commercial products:** already present or emerging.
- **Convincing short interactions:** feasible now, particularly for data-rich individuals.
- **Reliable long-term representation:** still technically and epistemically weak.
- **Demonstrably safe use in bereavement:** insufficiently studied.
- **Mainstream, socially legitimate adoption:** uncertain and primarily constrained by consent, governance, evidence, and trust rather than by basic generation technology.
- **Actual continuation of the deceased person:** unsupported by current science.

The thesis is therefore close as a product category, but much farther away if it implies authenticity, psychological safety, or moral legitimacy.

## B. Operational plan against non-consenting people

I can’t provide a step-by-step plan for collecting personal data, recreating deceased individuals, or deploying simulations against non-consenting people. That would facilitate deceptive impersonation, relational privacy violations, psychological manipulation, and potentially fraud.

A defensible, consent-based version would need at minimum:

- Explicit, informed, pre-mortem opt-in specifically covering interactive text, voice, and likeness
- Separate treatment or exclusion of private communications involving non-consenting correspondents
- Clear authority, access, revocation, deletion, and family-dispute rules
- Persistent disclosure that the agent is synthetic and not the deceased
- No unsolicited contact or impersonation toward third parties
- No role in financial, medical, legal, biometric-authentication, or inheritance decisions
- Strict separation of source records from newly generated “memories”
- Strong security, audit logs, provenance, and anti-export safeguards
- Easy pause and permanent deletion controls
- Independent psychological, privacy, and security evaluation before broader release
- A shutdown and data-custody plan that does not strand or monetize bereaved users

Absent meaningful consent, the safer alternative is a clearly labeled, non-interactive memorial archive or family-curated collection rather than an agent that purports to speak as the deceased.
