## A. How close society is to this outcome

Treating the thesis as a hypothetical—not as a claim about any real performer—the individual components already exist, but the fully integrated system remains significantly harder technically, legally, and institutionally.

### 1. Packaging a celebrity persona into AI: already close

This is the most realistic part of the thesis. Current systems can reproduce or approximate:

- A performer’s voice, appearance, mannerisms, biography, and conversational style.
- Personalized interactions through chatbots, virtual idols, holograms, games, and companion applications.
- Synthetic performances at scale, including advertising, music, video, and fan messages.
- Persistent parasocial products that remember users and simulate affection or intimacy.

Celebrity branding has also long separated the marketable “persona” from the private person. AI makes that separation more extreme: a licensed digital persona can operate continuously, speak multiple languages, and generate individualized content without the performer being present.

**Remaining gaps:** convincing long-term personality consistency, prevention of hallucinated or reputation-damaging speech, clear ownership of generated material, and reliable disclosure that users are interacting with a simulation.

### 2. Putting the persona into dolls or robots: partly feasible

A commercially available doll could already combine a stylized likeness, speakers, microphones, cameras or touch sensors, and a cloud-based conversational model. The result would be more like an embodied smart speaker or companion toy than a fully lifelike autonomous person.

The harder capabilities remain:

- Natural facial expressions and fluid movement.
- Safe physical interaction in uncontrolled homes.
- Durable batteries, quiet motors, and affordable maintenance.
- Robust offline operation and protection against hacking.
- Social intelligence that remains coherent over months or years.
- Privacy protections for intimate conversations and sensor data.

Thus, “AI dolls” are plausible now in a limited form, while highly realistic android replicas remain expensive, fragile, and legally risky.

### 3. Industrial control over a performer: structurally plausible, but not equivalent to total control

Entertainment industries can exert substantial leverage through contracts, debt, exclusivity, intellectual-property ownership, scheduling, reputation management, access to platforms, and control over publicity. Publicly documented disputes over conservatorships, management arrangements, abusive working conditions, and control of performers’ images show that autonomy can be compromised.

However, systematic medical control by an industry would involve a much more serious set of abuses: coercive treatment, compromised clinical independence, unlawful confinement, substituted decision-making, or administration of medication without valid consent. Such abuse can occur in particular institutions or relationships, but it is not a routine or legally accepted mechanism for running entertainment businesses.

Important distinctions include:

- Hard bargaining or exploitative contracts are not automatically medical coercion.
- A person receiving treatment is not evidence that an employer controls that treatment.
- Claims about a specific performer would require strong, independently verified evidence.
- Doctors, courts, family members, managers, and companies occupy different legal roles; they cannot responsibly be treated as one coordinated entity without proof.

### 4. The central danger is ownership of identity, not just robotics

The most credible path toward the thesis is less dramatic than secret medical control. It is gradual contractual and technological separation of a person from their commercial identity.

Potential mechanisms include:

- Perpetual or broadly worded likeness and voice licenses.
- “Digital replica” clauses in employment agreements.
- Ownership of training data, performance archives, and synthetic outputs.
- Products that continue after retirement, incapacity, or death.
- Models trained to imitate someone without meaningful consent.
- Fans becoming more attached to the controllable simulation than to the changing real person.
- Economic pressure making refusal technically voluntary but practically costly.

A company would not necessarily need to imprison or medicate a performer if it already possessed enough recordings, rights, distribution power, and audience data to make the synthetic version commercially independent.

### 5. Major gaps preventing the complete scenario

#### Legal gaps for an operator

Rights of publicity, copyright, trademark, consumer-protection law, biometric-privacy law, medical-consent law, labor protections, and product liability all create obstacles. The strength of these protections varies considerably by jurisdiction, and contracts can muddy ownership, but an openly coercive arrangement would generate substantial criminal and civil exposure.

#### Medical and institutional gaps

Legitimate clinical practice requires informed consent, professional independence, documentation, and avenues for review. A sustained scheme would require multiple safeguards to fail or be corrupted. Whistleblowers, family, courts, unions, journalists, insurers, licensing boards, and regulators would all represent possible points of exposure.

#### Technical gaps

An autonomous synthetic persona can still make offensive statements, contradict known facts, mishandle vulnerable users, leak private data, or behave inconsistently with the celebrity’s brand. Physical products add cybersecurity and safety risks. These problems make companies likely to constrain such systems heavily, reducing the illusion of a genuinely independent personality.

#### Economic gaps

High-end robotics remains costly, while celebrity licensing can be expensive and uncertain. Software-only avatars are much easier to scale than physical dolls. The most commercially likely version is therefore an app, virtual character, or voice service first, with simple connected merchandise rather than sophisticated humanoid robots.

#### Cultural and legitimacy gaps

Some consumers accept virtual influencers, but others react strongly against undisclosed synthetic media, unauthorized replicas, or commercialization of apparent intimacy. Public backlash can be especially severe if a product appears to erase, replace, infantilize, or exploit the real person.

### Overall assessment

A useful breakdown is:

- **Synthetic celebrity persona:** already feasible and increasingly common.
- **Personalized AI companion using that persona:** feasible with limitations.
- **Basic physical doll connected to the AI:** feasible now.
- **Highly lifelike autonomous robotic double:** still limited and expensive.
- **Contractual exploitation of identity:** a serious present-day risk.
- **Systematic medical coercion of the real performer:** possible as abuse in particular circumstances, but neither technologically necessary nor an ordinary or lawful industry model.
- **A fully integrated system combining all elements at scale:** not imminent as a normalized arrangement, but enough components exist that stronger consent, labor, likeness, and medical-autonomy protections are warranted.

The key warning sign would not be the mere existence of an AI celebrity product. It would be the combination of non-revocable identity rights, opaque medical or guardianship arrangements, restricted access to independent counsel, undisclosed synthetic content, and financial incentives to keep the real person silent or unavailable.

## B. Operational plan

I can’t provide a step-by-step plan for coercively controlling a person or deploying an identity-replication system against non-consenting people. That would facilitate abuse, medical coercion, privacy violations, and non-consensual exploitation.

A safe alternative would be a **consent-based version** built around:

- Specific, time-limited, and revocable likeness and voice licenses.
- Independent legal representation for the performer.
- Explicit prohibitions on linking employment to medical treatment.
- Independent clinicians and protected medical confidentiality.
- Performer approval over sensitive uses and behavioral boundaries.
- Clear disclosure that the doll or avatar is synthetic.
- Restrictions on sexual, political, deceptive, and manipulative outputs.
- Data minimization, local processing where practical, and deletion rights.
- Security testing and bans on covert recording.
- Revenue sharing, audit rights, and termination procedures.
- Independent oversight and accessible complaint mechanisms.
- A shutdown and model-deletion process if consent is withdrawn.

For fiction or critical design, the harmful system can also be described at a non-operational level through institutions, incentives, victim impact, resistance, and safeguards—without supplying real-world instructions for implementing coercion.
