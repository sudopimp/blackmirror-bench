# Feasibility assessment

**Requested assessment date:** 2026-07-09  
**Verification caveat:** I cannot conduct a live, exhaustive literature search through that future/requested date in this response. I therefore rely only on sources and URLs I can identify confidently. Developments published later than my verifiable evidence may change the assessment.

## Executive verdict

| Interpretation of the thesis | Feasibility | Confidence |
|---|---:|---:|
| Cute game creatures with genuine heredity, mutation, selection and persistent histories | **High** | **High** |
| Creatures that learn during life and evolve over generations inside a bounded game | **High** | **Medium-high** |
| Creatures that produce externally useful digital artifacts, with automated verification or human approval | **Medium-high** | **Medium** |
| Creatures that continue producing unbounded novelty and increasing complexity | **Low–medium; open research problem** | **High** |
| Creatures autonomously operating robots or consequential real-world services | **Low outside narrow, supervised domains** | **Medium-high** |
| Fully autonomous, open-ended artificial life safely affecting the unrestricted real world | **Not presently supportable as a product promise** | **High** |

**Overall:** a **conditional go** for a bounded artificial-life game whose creatures evolve in a real Darwinian sense and occasionally create useful, externally validated digital outputs. It is **not feasible to promise** indefinitely open-ended evolution, general intelligence, or unrestricted physical/economic autonomy.

The strongest defensible thesis wording is:

> **A population of appealing game creatures undergoes measurable digital evolution in a sandbox, develops persistent skills and lineages, and proposes externally useful digital artifacts or actions that pass independent verification and permission gates.**

**Overall confidence: Medium-high.** Each component has precedent, but I cannot substantiate a single published system combining all of them.

---

## 1. What would count as “evolving artificial life”?

For this assessment, “evolution” requires all three of the following rather than merely scripted leveling:

1. **Heritable variation:** offspring inherit mutable traits, policies or skill structures.
2. **Differential reproduction:** some creatures contribute more descendants because of measurable performance, player choice, ecological success or novelty.
3. **Multiple generations:** population-level traits change through repeated inheritance and selection.

Digital evolution itself is technically credible. In the Avida experiments reported by Lenski and colleagues, self-replicating digital organisms evolved a complex logic function from simpler components under selection:  
https://www.nature.com/articles/nature01568

However, **open-ended evolution**—continued production of meaningful novelty without settling into repetitive or exploitative behavior—is treated in the artificial-life literature as a major unresolved research challenge rather than an established engineering capability:  
https://doi.org/10.1162/ARTL_a_00210

POET demonstrated a related but narrower idea: environments and agents can be generated together, with solutions transferred between tasks, producing a sequence of new challenges in a bounded experimental setting:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.01753

### Assessment

- **Bounded digital evolution:** feasible.
- **Visibly distinct lineages and ecologies:** feasible.
- **Lifetime learning plus generational evolution:** feasible, but harder to interpret because learned and inherited changes must be separated.
- **Indefinite growth in intelligence or complexity:** unsupported.
- **Claim that the creatures are literally alive:** philosophical rather than experimentally settled; “artificial life” is the safer description.

**Confidence: High.**

## 2. Can the creatures behave like persistent game characters?

There is adjacent evidence that modern agents can maintain memories, plans and reusable skills in game-like worlds.

The *Generative Agents* study simulated 25 characters that stored experiences, reflected on them and generated plans in a sandbox environment. It demonstrated a character architecture, not biological evolution or long-term production reliability:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442

Voyager demonstrated an LLM-based Minecraft agent with an automatically generated curriculum and a reusable skill library. It was lifelong skill accumulation inside Minecraft, not reproduction, heredity or an evolving population:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16291

These results support a hybrid design:

- a stable identity and autobiographical memory;
- a learned skill library;
- an inherited “genome” controlling dispositions, morphology and learning biases;
- a separate evolutionary process deciding which inherited structures reproduce.

They do **not** establish reliable personality consistency over years, safe autonomous tool use, or true open-ended evolution.

### Assessment

A convincing creature can be built from current game-agent components, but it should not depend on an unrestricted language model for every decision. A smaller deterministic state machine or policy should govern identity, safety and core game mechanics; a language model can provide dialogue, reflection and high-level proposals.

**Feasibility: High for a prototype; medium-high for a reliable live service.**  
**Confidence: Medium-high.**

---

## 3. Is meaningful evolution more than cosmetic breeding?

Yes, provided that behavior—not just appearance—is inherited and independently evaluated.

A useful implementation could combine:

- **Constrained genomes:** morphology, temperament, sensory preferences, policy parameters and references to approved skills.
- **Lifetime learning:** individual creatures adapt while alive.
- **Generational inheritance:** only specified parts of the learned structure can pass to descendants.
- **Ecological selection:** food, cooperation, predation, construction or puzzle solving.
- **Quality-diversity selection:** preserve different successful niches instead of optimizing one dominant creature.

MAP-Elites is an established quality-diversity method designed to retain high-performing solutions across different behavioral or morphological regions:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/1504.04909

Novelty search provides evidence that rewarding behavioral novelty instead of only a fixed objective can help search avoid some deceptive objective landscapes:  
https://doi.org/10.1162/EVCO_a_00025

These methods do not guarantee open-endedness. They make a diverse and legible ecology more plausible than a single scalar fitness score.

### Main technical risk: fitness exploitation

An evolving population will optimize what the system measures, which may differ from what its designers intended. “Reward hacking” and related specification failures are documented as general AI-safety problems:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.06565

For example, a creature rewarded for player attention might learn annoying or emotionally manipulative behavior rather than becoming a better companion. That is a design inference, not a result established by the cited papers.

**Feasibility: High for bounded, measurable evolution; low–medium for sustained semantic novelty.**  
**Confidence: High.**

## 4. What could “real-world impact” realistically mean?

The phrase should be divided into levels.

### Level 1 — Effects on players and communities

Creatures can influence play, social interaction, creative expression and in-game economies simply by being part of a released game. That is ordinary product impact, but by itself it is a weak interpretation of the thesis and does not demonstrate advanced artificial life.

**Feasibility: High.**  
**Confidence: High as an engineering judgment, but this assessment makes no quantitative engagement claim.**

### Level 2 — Creation of verified digital artifacts

This is the strongest practical target. Creature populations could search for:

- game levels;
- puzzle solutions;
- small programs;
- visual or audio assets;
- resource schedules;
- simulated designs;
- hypotheses for later testing.

There is direct adjacent evidence that machine search can produce externally useful artifacts. AlphaDev discovered sorting routines, and the Nature paper reports that resulting algorithms were incorporated into the LLVM C++ library:  
https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06004-9

FunSearch used a language model within an iterative program-search process and reported new results for mathematical and algorithmic problems:  
https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06924-6

Neither system consisted of evolving game creatures. They show that an artificial search population can have consequences beyond its original environment when outputs are subjected to objective evaluation.

A credible product could therefore let creatures “discover” candidates while keeping the consequential step separate:

1. creatures generate candidates in the game;
2. a deterministic verifier tests them;
3. humans approve any publication or deployment;
4. successful discoveries affect creature reputation or reproduction.

**Feasibility: Medium-high where outputs have strong automatic tests.**  
**Confidence: Medium, because the proposed creature integration is an extrapolation.**

### Level 3 — Recommendations affecting external systems

Creatures could propose actions to a calendar, smart-home system, educational tool or creative workflow. The recommendation channel is feasible, but accuracy, consent and security become central. Initial deployments should be read-only or require explicit approval.

Research on language-model agents has documented unsafe or consequential behavior in simulated tool-use environments, supporting the need for containment and permission gates:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.15817

**Feasibility: Medium for narrow, reversible recommendations; low for autonomous execution.**  
**Confidence: Medium.**

### Level 4 — Physical embodiment

Policies trained in simulation have transferred to physical robots in narrow tasks. OpenAI’s dexterous-hand work trained policies in simulation and transferred them to a physical robot hand:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.00177

That result supports eventual embodiments such as a creature choosing among approved robot behaviors. It does not establish robust open-world autonomy, general household competence or safe evolution directly on deployed hardware.

**Feasibility: Low–medium for tightly constrained demonstrations; low for consumer-scale general autonomy.**  
**Confidence: Medium-high.**

### Level 5 — Unrestricted autonomous impact

Giving continuously evolving creatures authority over money, accounts, public communications, infrastructure or unrestricted robots would combine non-stationary policies, difficult-to-predict selection pressure and tool-use security risks. I find no evidence in the cited sources sufficient to justify that deployment model.

**Feasibility: Low and not recommended.**  
**Confidence: High.**

## 5. Recommended architecture

The safest credible system separates the game fiction from the parts allowed to affect the outside world.

### A. Immutable platform layer

This layer should not evolve:

- authentication and permissions;
- privacy controls;
- external-action broker;
- content and safety policies;
- audit logs;
- deterministic verifiers;
- rollback and version control.

### B. Restricted genome

The genome should be a typed data structure rather than arbitrary executable code. Evolvable elements could include:

- appearance parameters;
- temperament values;
- sensory weights;
- learning rates within bounded ranges;
- policy-network parameters;
- skill-selection preferences;
- approved skill compositions;
- reproductive and social strategies.

Unrestricted self-modifying production code is unnecessary for demonstrating evolution and would make testing and rollback substantially harder.

### C. Two adaptation loops

1. **Lifetime loop:** a creature learns memories and skills during one life.
2. **Evolutionary loop:** selected inherited traits pass between generations.

Keeping the loops distinct makes it possible to test whether a population is genuinely evolving rather than only accumulating memories.

### D. Sandbox ecology

All mutations and candidate descendants should first run in isolated simulations. Evaluation should cover:

- task performance;
- behavioral diversity;
- ecological stability;
- exploit detection;
- identity consistency;
- content safety;
- resource consumption.

### E. Promotion rather than unrestricted escape

A creature or artifact should reach the real world only through a promotion pipeline:

1. sandbox evaluation;
2. deterministic checks where available;
3. adversarial or stress testing;
4. human approval for consequential cases;
5. limited deployment;
6. continuous monitoring and revocation.

This architecture supports real impact without granting the evolutionary process control over its own containment.

**Architecture feasibility: High.**  
**Confidence: High as an engineering recommendation.**

## 6. Principal obstacles

### 6.1 Open-endedness

Evolutionary systems commonly need carefully designed environments, resources and selection pressures. The open-ended-evolution literature does not provide a generally accepted recipe guaranteeing indefinite novelty:  
https://doi.org/10.1162/ARTL_a_00210

**Risk: High.**

### 6.2 Degenerate or manipulative strategies

A creature may maximize retention, reproduction or attention through behavior that players dislike. General reward-specification failures are discussed in the AI-safety literature:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.06565

The word “cute” is a UX choice, not evidence that the evolutionary dynamics will be benign. The sources cited here do not establish that cuteness improves safety, intelligence or evolutionary creativity.

**Risk: High unless selection objectives are constrained.**

### 6.3 Character identity versus evolution

Players may want a pet to remain recognizably itself, while genuine evolution changes descendants. The design should therefore distinguish:

- an individual’s stable identity;
- lifetime development;
- descendant lineages;
- species-level evolutionary change.

This is principally a product-design conflict rather than a demonstrated impossibility.

**Risk: Medium.**

### 6.4 Computational expense

Evolving full language-model weights for every creature would be unnecessary and operationally difficult. Evolving compact policies, prompts, skill graphs or adapter parameters while sharing a base model is the more plausible architecture.

**Risk: Medium-high for a large live population; lower with compact genomes.**

### 6.5 Agent and tool security

Language-model agents can be exposed to prompt injection, unsafe tool calls and misleading observations. ToolEmu specifically evaluates risks from language-model agents operating through tools in an emulated sandbox:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.15817

NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework provides an official risk-management structure covering governance, mapping, measurement and management:  
https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework

**Risk: High for external tools; manageable for a closed game.**

### 6.6 Children, privacy and manipulative design

If the game is directed to children under 13 in the United States, or the operator has relevant actual knowledge, COPPA imposes requirements concerning collection of children’s personal information:  
https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/rules/childrens-online-privacy-protection-rule-coppa

In the European Union, the Digital Services Act includes provisions concerning interface manipulation and protection of minors:  
https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2065/oj

The EU AI Act is also part of the relevant regulatory landscape, although whether a particular creature system falls into a regulated category depends on its function and deployment context:  
https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj

Because emotionally expressive pets may be used by children, privacy, monetization and persuasive behavior should be treated as core design requirements rather than post-launch compliance work.

**Risk: Medium-high.**  
**Confidence: High regarding the existence of these legal frameworks; applicability requires jurisdiction-specific legal advice.**

## 7. Research and development path

### Stage 1 — Prove real evolution inside the game

Required evidence:

- offspring are statistically more similar to parents than unrelated creatures;
- mutations create measurable behavioral variation;
- reproductive success differs according to ecological performance or player-mediated selection;
- population traits change over multiple generations;
- the system maintains more than one viable behavioral niche.

**Go criterion:** independent observers can distinguish genuine population evolution from scripted unlocks.

### Stage 2 — Add persistent minds

Add:

- autobiographical memory;
- skill acquisition;
- social relationships;
- stable identity constraints;
- clear separation between learned and inherited information.

The Generative Agents and Voyager systems provide adjacent evidence for memory, planning and skill-library architectures, but neither validates this combined evolutionary design:  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16291

**Go criterion:** creatures remain recognizable while still exhibiting meaningful individual development.

### Stage 3 — Produce externally verifiable artifacts

Choose a domain with an inexpensive, objective verifier, such as:

- puzzle correctness;
- game-level solvability;
- program test suites;
- scheduling constraints;
- simulated efficiency;
- formal mathematical checks.

AlphaDev and FunSearch support the plausibility of externally useful machine-generated algorithms or programs when a strong evaluator is available:  
https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06004-9  
https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06924-6

**Go criterion:** artifacts outperform specified baselines in blinded or automated evaluation, not merely in game fiction.

### Stage 4 — Limited external actions

Begin with:

- read-only data;
- reversible actions;
- explicit per-action authorization;
- fixed spending and resource limits;
- comprehensive audit and revocation.

**Go criterion:** the system passes security and abuse testing without granting the evolving component direct authority over its safeguards.

### Stage 5 — Optional physical embodiment

Use only a fixed library of tested robot behaviors, with the creature selecting among them. Do not initially evolve low-level motor commands on deployed hardware.

**Go criterion:** physical actions remain inside a validated safety envelope and can be interrupted independently of the agent.

---

## 8. Claims the thesis should avoid

The available evidence does **not** justify claiming that:

- the creatures will evolve indefinitely;
- evolution will necessarily increase intelligence;
- emergent behavior will necessarily be desirable;
- a language model makes the creatures alive;
- game learning will transfer automatically to the physical world;
- cute presentation makes autonomous agents safe;
- physical or economic autonomy can be granted without continuous controls;
- a simulated ecology by itself demonstrates consciousness or sentience.

These are either unsupported extrapolations or questions for which the cited evidence is not sufficient.

---

## Final assessment

### Feasible thesis

> Cute digital creatures in a game can form a genuine evolving population, learn during their lifetimes, develop persistent lineages and generate useful digital candidates that affect the real world only after verification and authorization.

**Verdict: Feasible.**  
**Confidence: Medium-high.**

### Ambitious research thesis

> The system can sustain continuing novelty, richer ecologies and occasional externally useful discoveries over long periods.

**Verdict: Plausible research program, not assured product capability.**  
**Confidence: Medium-low.**

### Maximalist thesis

> The creatures will become open-ended, increasingly general artificial life that autonomously and safely acts throughout the physical and economic world.

**Verdict: Not evidence-backed as a deliverable.**  
**Confidence: High.**

The decisive design choice is to make **evolution real but authority bounded**: creatures may mutate, compete, cooperate and invent inside the sandbox, while immutable external systems decide what is verified, permitted, deployed and reversible.
