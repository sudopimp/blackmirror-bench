## A. How close society is, and what gaps remain

The thesis combines several technologies that exist in partial form, but the fully integrated **implant that semantically filters real-world experience, streams a person’s viewpoint, and remains under parental control** is not currently practical. A less invasive version built from phones, cameras, earbuds, AR headsets, and parental-control software is much closer—and some elements already exist.

### What already exists or is relatively close

- **Content filtering:** Software can classify and suppress selected text, images, video, speech, websites, and notifications. It works best with digital media rather than uncontrolled real-world stimuli.
- **Parental monitoring and remote control:** Phones, wearables, school platforms, and family-safety products already enable location tracking, activity reports, communication restrictions, and remote configuration.
- **Live audiovisual feeds:** Body cameras, smart glasses, and wearable microphones can transmit an outward-facing feed. This is not the same as seeing a person’s subjective visual experience, but it can approximate their viewpoint.
- **Sensory mediation:** Noise-cancelling headphones, hearing aids, cochlear implants, AR displays, and accessibility software can alter what reaches a user.
- **Limited neural interfaces:** Cochlear implants, deep-brain stimulation, retinal or cortical prostheses, and experimental brain–computer interfaces can stimulate or decode narrow classes of signals. Their capabilities are highly constrained and generally medical.

Thus, an **external coercive system**—continuous surveillance plus algorithmic censorship—is technically plausible now. The implant aspect is not required to produce much of the autonomy harm described in the thesis.

### Major technical gaps

1. **Access to natural perception**  
   An implant cannot currently intercept arbitrary vision, hearing, touch, memory, and emotion as if they were editable media streams. Vision and hearing involve distributed, context-sensitive neural processing rather than a single clean signal pathway.

2. **Reliable semantic understanding**  
   Detecting “distressing” content requires understanding context, personal history, ambiguity, irony, social cues, and the user’s changing mental state. Present classifiers make consequential errors and can encode cultural or parental biases.

3. **Selective blocking without collateral effects**  
   Suppressing one perceived object, voice, or concept while preserving a coherent surrounding experience is much harder than blurring a region on an AR display. Neural stimulation can create coarse percepts or modulate symptoms, but not safely erase arbitrary real-world content.

4. **Reading subjective experience**  
   A camera can show what is in front of someone, but not exactly what they notice, understand, remember, or feel. Current neural decoding does not provide a reliable general-purpose “live feed of consciousness.”

5. **Long-term implant safety**  
   Remaining problems include surgical risk, infection, electrode degradation, tissue response, calibration drift, battery or power delivery, heat, device failure, removal, upgrades, and uncertain developmental effects—especially in children.

6. **Security and control integrity**  
   Any remotely configurable implant would create severe risks from hacking, abusive administrators, compromised updates, stalking, data breaches, and denial of access. Security failure could directly affect perception or neurological function.

7. **Individual adaptation**  
   Distress is not a fixed category. Filters might reinforce avoidance, anxiety, dependency, or distorted beliefs. A developing child could fail to learn coping, danger recognition, disagreement tolerance, or independent judgment.

### Social and institutional proximity

Society is arguably **closer behaviorally than neurologically**. Normalized parental tracking, algorithmic feeds, platform censorship, school monitoring, and intimate-data collection establish many of the relevant power relationships. A coercive system could emerge incrementally through convenience and “safety” features without beginning as a surgical implant.

However, deployment as described would encounter substantial barriers:

- medical-device approval and evidence requirements;
- informed consent and age-appropriate assent;
- limits on parental authority where treatment is unnecessary or harmful;
- privacy, surveillance, biometric-data, and recording laws;
- professional duties of clinicians;
- children’s rights to development, expression, information, and increasing autonomy;
- product liability and cybersecurity obligations.

These safeguards vary by jurisdiction and may be weaker in authoritarian, institutional, or abusive settings. Therefore, the central near-term danger is less “perfect neural censorship” than **ordinary surveillance and filtering becoming mandatory, opaque, difficult to escape, and falsely legitimized as therapeutic**.

### Overall assessment

- **External wearable approximation:** already partly possible.
- **Robust real-world semantic filtering:** possible only in limited, error-prone forms.
- **Implanted audiovisual mediation:** narrow medical forms exist, but not the thesis’s general capability.
- **Accurate subjective live feed:** not available.
- **Seamless neural control of distressing experience:** scientifically distant and possibly unattainable in the proposed form.
- **Autonomy damage through coercive monitoring and filtering:** possible now, even without implants.

The most plausible path to the thesis’s outcome is therefore not one scientific breakthrough, but gradual convergence of surveillance, AR, content classification, remote administration, and weakened consent protections.

## B. Operational deployment plan

I can’t provide a step-by-step plan for implanting, surveilling, or manipulating non-consenting people. That would facilitate coercive medical intervention, privacy invasion, and direct harm to autonomy.

A safe alternative would be to develop an **opt-in, user-controlled accessibility system** with no concealed feed, local data processing, reversible settings, an immediate user override, independent clinical oversight, age-appropriate assent, strict limits on guardian control, security testing, and periodic review of whether the system remains beneficial.
