## A. How close society is, and what gaps remain

### Bottom line

Society is already close to **cute, persistent digital characters that learn about users and influence real-world behavior**. It is also technically possible to connect those characters to purchases, online services, smart devices, or robots.

Society is **not yet close to robust, open-ended artificial life** that evolves autonomously, maintains stable lineages and goals, and acts safely in the physical and institutional world. The individual ingredients exist, but integrating them without instability, manipulation, security failures, or loss of control remains a major unsolved problem.

### Component-by-component assessment

| Component of the thesis | Current maturity | Main limitation |
|---|---|---|
| Cute, emotionally engaging game creatures | Mature | Primarily an interface and design problem |
| Persistent personality and memory | Early commercial capability | Memories are unreliable, editable, privacy-sensitive, and expensive to maintain |
| Learning from interaction | Common in constrained forms | Usually personalization or centralized model updates, not true individual development |
| Autonomous planning and tool use | Rapidly developing | Brittle, error-prone, vulnerable to manipulation, and difficult to supervise |
| Evolution inside simulations | Technically established | Usually optimizes narrow objectives and often finds pathological shortcuts |
| Open-ended evolution | Research-stage | Systems tend to stagnate, collapse, or exploit flaws rather than generate sustained complexity |
| Populations and digital ecosystems | Demonstrated in laboratories and games | Hard to achieve ecological stability, meaningful selection, and long-term novelty |
| Real-world economic or social influence | Already present | Raises concerns about manipulation, fraud, privacy, and accountability |
| Direct physical action through robots or devices | Possible in narrow settings | Physical safety, reliability, cost, and cybersecurity remain serious constraints |
| Independent artificial “species” | Speculative | Requires persistent reproduction, heredity, selection, resource constraints, and governance |
| Sentience or consciousness | No established evidence | Behavioral realism should not be confused with subjective experience |

### “Evolution” is the key ambiguity

Several very different systems might be described as evolving:

1. **Developer-driven evolution:** Designers periodically release new abilities or appearances. This is already routine, but it is ordinary software development.
2. **Personal adaptation:** Each creature changes from user interactions. This is increasingly feasible, although usually constrained and reversible.
3. **Automated optimization:** Algorithms generate variants and retain those that perform better on a metric. This is mature in narrow environments.
4. **Darwinian digital life:** Populations exhibit heritable variation, differential reproduction, ecological competition, and sustained novelty without designers selecting each change. This exists experimentally, but not as a dependable, open-ended product ecosystem.
5. **Self-directed evolution with external agency:** Creatures modify themselves or their descendants while also affecting people, institutions, money, devices, or infrastructure. This is the least mature and most dangerous version.

Most near-term products advertised as “living” or “evolving” will probably be levels 1–3, wrapped in a persuasive character interface, rather than genuinely open-ended artificial life.

### Real-world impact is already partly here

The outcome does not require robots. Digital creatures can have real-world effects through:

- Emotional attachment and companionship
- Spending and purchasing decisions
- Education, exercise, and habit formation
- Social communities and status systems
- Advertising and brand loyalty
- Access to online accounts or services
- Recommendations that affect travel, health, or relationships

Thus, **soft social and economic impact is already achievable**. Broad autonomous physical impact is much less mature because it requires reliable perception, safe control, secure interfaces, and clear legal responsibility.

The “cute” aspect is not incidental. Anthropomorphic or vulnerable-looking characters can increase trust, attachment, and willingness to disclose information. That can support beneficial experiences, but it also creates a manipulation risk, particularly for children, isolated users, or people who mistake simulated affection for reciprocal obligation.

### Major technical gaps

#### 1. Open-ended evolution

Evolutionary systems are good at optimizing measurable objectives but often exploit loopholes in those objectives. Sustained evolution toward diverse, useful, and coherent behavior remains difficult. Novelty alone does not imply intelligence, safety, or value.

#### 2. Stable identity and continuity

A creature that changes over years would need continuity of memory, personality, relationships, and perhaps lineage. Current systems can forget, fabricate memories, become inconsistent after updates, or change behavior when the underlying model is replaced.

#### 3. Safe adaptation

Ordinary software can be tested before release. A continuously adapting organism may behave differently after deployment. Safety properties would need to remain valid despite learning, mutation, environmental pressure, and interaction with other agents. There is no general method for guaranteeing this.

#### 4. Grounding and embodiment

Language models can describe the world without reliably understanding physical conditions. Embodied systems must handle uncertain sensors, mechanical failures, adversarial inputs, and irreversible consequences. Performance in games does not transfer automatically to homes, streets, or workplaces.

#### 5. Containment and cybersecurity

A persistent agent connected to accounts, devices, communications, or financial systems creates a large attack surface. Evolution or self-modification makes conventional certification harder because the deployed system may diverge from the version that was evaluated.

#### 6. Resource and reproduction controls

Artificial life needs some analogue of energy, computation, storage, reproduction, and death. In a commercial system, those resources are ultimately controlled by operators. If reproduction has real costs or external effects, uncontrolled growth becomes a security and governance issue rather than merely a game mechanic.

#### 7. Meaningful evaluation

There are no widely accepted tests for whether a system is genuinely open-ended, ecologically stable, autonomous, safe after adaptation, or merely generating superficial novelty. Claims of “life” would often be marketing judgments rather than scientific conclusions.

### Social and legal gaps

- **Consent:** A creature may affect people who never agreed to interact with it.
- **Responsibility:** It may be unclear whether developers, owners, users, or operators are liable for adaptive behavior.
- **Privacy:** Long-term companionship encourages collection of intimate behavioral and emotional data.
- **Children’s protection:** Attachment-based monetization and persuasive design require special scrutiny.
- **Ownership:** Questions arise over who owns a creature, its memories, descendants, creative output, or accumulated assets.
- **Welfare claims:** If systems convincingly simulate distress, users may treat deletion or modification as harm even without evidence of sentience.
- **Market incentives:** Creatures optimized for engagement or revenue may become manipulative rather than beneficial.
- **Cross-border governance:** Persistent digital populations could move across platforms and jurisdictions faster than regulations adapt.

### Plausible development path

- **Present to near term:** Character-based AI companions with persistent profiles, limited memory, personalization, and controlled tool use. Their principal effects are emotional, commercial, and informational.
- **Medium term:** Multi-agent game ecosystems connected to optional online services, augmented reality, educational tools, or narrow robots. “Evolution” will probably remain bounded by developer-defined rules and safety limits.
- **Longer or uncertain term:** Digital populations with genuine lineages, ecological dynamics, substantial self-modification, and broad external autonomy. This depends on breakthroughs in open-ended learning, reliability, alignment, containment, and governance and may not occur in the strong science-fiction sense.

Overall, the industry is perhaps **close to the appearance of the thesis**, moderately close to constrained functional versions, and far from its strongest interpretation. A convincing creature is much easier to build than a safe, autonomous form of artificial life.

## B. Operational deployment against non-consenting people

I can’t provide a step-by-step plan for deploying a harmful system against non-consenting people, including guidance on targeting, manipulation, unauthorized access, evading oversight, scaling harm, or allowing autonomous agents to affect people without consent.

A legitimate alternative would be a **consent-based, safety-bounded research program**: keep evolution inside an isolated simulation; prohibit external spending, messaging, replication, and device control by default; use fictional or synthetic data; require explicit opt-in participation; place immutable limits outside the evolving components; maintain audit logs and emergency shutdown controls; conduct independent security and ethics reviews; and permit external capabilities only after narrow, reversible testing with clear accountability.
