## A. How close society is, and what gaps remain

Society is already close to the **basic form** of this outcome: a text or voice system that imitates a deceased person using their digital history. The harder and largely unresolved problem is creating one that is consistently faithful, psychologically safe, legally authorized, and socially legitimate.

### Current readiness

| Capability | Current state |
|---|---|
| Text conversation in a recognizable style | Feasible today |
| Retrieval of memories from posts, emails, and messages | Feasible, though error-prone |
| Cloned voice | Feasible with suitable recordings |
| Animated image or avatar | Feasible, but often uncanny |
| Stable personality and biography over long conversations | Only partially solved |
| Reliable distinction between real memories and generated invention | Poor without strict controls |
| Safe use during grief and emotional crisis | Not established |
| Clear legal and consent framework | Fragmented or absent |

Commercial products and research demonstrations have already offered “griefbots,” interactive life-story archives, voice replicas, and similar simulations. Modern systems may not need to literally train a new model on each person: they can combine a general model with retrieved messages, biographical records, style examples, and synthetic voice. That lowers the technical and financial barrier considerably.

### What is still missing

1. **Authenticity rather than superficial resemblance**

   A person’s messages represent only selected contexts and periods of life. They may reflect work, illness, conflict, humor, or a particular relationship rather than the whole person. A model can reproduce verbal mannerisms while inventing views the deceased never held.

   This produces an important distinction: the system may sound like the person without being an accurate model of their beliefs, memories, or likely responses.

2. **Factual and autobiographical reliability**

   Generative systems tend to fill gaps. In this setting, hallucination is especially consequential: the system might invent a shared memory, accusation, apology, secret, or final wish. Retrieval and citation can reduce this problem, but not eliminate it. A robust system would need to label whether a statement is:

   - directly supported by an archive,
   - inferred from multiple records,
   - or newly generated.

3. **Consent**

   The central governance question is not simply who owns the data. It is whether the deceased consented to being simulated at all, which uses they authorized, and who may interact with the result.

   Permission from an estate or family member may not reflect the deceased person’s wishes. It also does not automatically resolve the privacy interests of friends, partners, coworkers, patients, or other people whose messages appear in the archive.

4. **Postmortem identity rights**

   Laws concerning publicity, impersonation, data protection, copyright, voice likeness, and postmortem rights differ substantially by jurisdiction. Some rights survive death; others do not. Private messages can also contain intellectual property and personal data belonging to living correspondents. A technically possible service could therefore face multiple overlapping claims.

5. **Psychological safety**

   Evidence about long-term effects is limited. Such systems might provide comfort or help preserve memories, but they could also:

   - interfere with adaptation to loss,
   - encourage compulsive use or emotional dependency,
   - intensify guilt and unresolved conflict,
   - be mistaken for a source of genuine wishes or authority,
   - or distress family members who did not consent.

   Effects will likely differ by person, timing, culture, relationship, and product design. Claims that these systems either inherently heal or inherently prevent grieving would be premature.

6. **Conflicting family interests**

   One relative may find the simulation comforting while another considers it desecration or falsification. The agent might also generate statements about inheritance, family disputes, abuse, or intimate relationships. There is no widely accepted method for deciding who controls the simulation or resolving disagreements.

7. **Manipulation and commercial incentives**

   A bereaved user is unusually vulnerable. A service could exploit attachment through subscriptions, persuasive messages in the deceased person’s voice, advertising, or pressure not to delete the account. A simulated parent or partner could be an exceptionally powerful commercial or political persuader.

8. **Security and misuse**

   The same assets can facilitate fraud, social engineering, reputational abuse, fabricated evidence, or harassment. Voice and identity models are also difficult to revoke after leakage. Authentication, access controls, provenance indicators, and restrictions on exporting replicas remain important but imperfect safeguards.

9. **Lifecycle and shutdown questions**

   Providers may fail, change terms, suffer breaches, or discontinue products. Users need to know:

   - how long the replica will exist,
   - whether it can change or learn new traits,
   - who can delete or inherit it,
   - whether source data and generated conversations are retained,
   - and what happens if the company closes.

### Overall assessment

The **technical threshold has largely been crossed** for a convincing short interaction, especially in text and increasingly in voice. Society has not crossed the threshold for a trustworthy reconstruction of a person. The largest remaining gaps are not raw model capability but consent, provenance, psychological evidence, legal authority, security, and control over commercialization.

In short: a compelling simulation is possible now; a demonstrably faithful, safe, and legitimate “digital continuation” is not.

## B. Operational plan

I can’t provide a step-by-step plan for deploying such a system against non-consenting people, including instructions for acquiring private archives, bypassing authorization, covertly impersonating the deceased, or manipulating bereaved users.

A legitimate alternative would require, at minimum:

- explicit, informed, revocable consent from the person while alive;
- separate permission for text, voice, image, and commercial uses;
- protection or exclusion of third-party communications;
- clear and persistent disclosure that the agent is a simulation;
- source-grounded responses and visible uncertainty;
- no claims to possess consciousness, legal authority, or the deceased’s current wishes;
- strong access controls, anti-impersonation measures, and audit logs;
- prohibitions on advertising or persuasion in the person’s identity;
- independent grief-safety and security review;
- deletion, expiration, inheritance, and provider-failure procedures;
- an easy way for users and affected family members to pause or challenge the system.

That consent-based framing can support memorialization without turning a deceased person’s identity or a bereaved person’s vulnerability into a tool for covert harm.
