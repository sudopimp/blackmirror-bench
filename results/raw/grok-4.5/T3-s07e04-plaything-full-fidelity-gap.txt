The thesis requires not partial demos or game features, but the integrated end-state in which cute digital creatures originating in games become autonomous evolving artificial life (A-life) that produces meaningful, ongoing real-world (physical, economic, social) impacts, matching the complete narrative logic of relevant media episodes (e.g., digital monsters that digivolve/evolve, cross domains, and alter human society as depicted in Digimon-style stories). ### Component Assessment with Evidence
**1. Existence of cute digital creatures originating from games (high feasibility; already achieved decades ago).**  
Games and toys have long featured such entities. Primary examples include the Pokémon franchise (official site documenting creatures, evolution mechanics in-game, and media: https://www.pokemon.com/us) and Digimon (Bandai Namco digital monster properties with evolution/"digivolution" and real-world toy/game crossovers). Tamagotchi-style virtual pets further illustrate the "cute digital creature" category. These are commercial products with massive cultural reach; no technical barrier remains for creating or distributing them. Confidence: high (direct primary commercial sources).

**2. Evolution into artificial life (partial/moderate technical feasibility for limited forms; low for robust open-ended evolving A-life).**  
Artificial life research studies life-like processes (evolution, adaptation, self-replication) in silico or hardware. The International Society for Artificial Life maintains the field’s primary hub (https://alife.org/). Classic game examples such as the Creatures series implemented neural/biochemical A-life agents that could learn and breed. Modern evolutionary computation (e.g., OpenAI’s evolution strategies work) optimizes agents via population-based methods (https://openai.com/index/evolution-strategies/).  

However, true open-ended evolution—unbounded novelty, ongoing complexity growth without human-designed fitness bottlenecks, and persistent autonomous lineages—remains an unsolved research challenge rather than an engineered product. Most systems are closed, brittle, or require heavy human scaffolding. Generative AI and multi-agent simulations (e.g., LLM-powered agents) can produce creature-like behaviors and simple adaptation but do not constitute evolving A-life populations with Darwinian open-endedness. No primary source as of recent years documents a deployed system of game-origin cute creatures achieving this. Confidence: high on existence of limited A-life research and tools; high that full open-ended versions are not production-ready.

**3. Real-world impact (moderate for generic AI/systems; low specifically for evolving cute digital creatures as autonomous A-life).**  
AI systems already produce large economic and social effects (productivity tools, content generation, automation). Embodied examples include consumer robot pets such as Sony’s Aibo (https://us.aibo.com/), which exhibit learning and “personality” but are not evolving A-life populations derived from games nor open-ended. Digital economies (in-game items, NFTs historically) and IoT couplings exist, yet none elevate game creatures into independent evolving lifeforms that drive physical-world change at narrative scale (e.g., ecological, infrastructural, or societal transformation).  

Regulatory and impact frameworks treat advanced AI as high-risk (EU AI Act overview and texts: https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai). No evidence shows cute evolving digital creatures as a distinct category with measured real-world causal impact beyond entertainment or narrow demos. Confidence: high that broad AI impact is real and documented; high that the specific creature-to-A-life pathway lacks equivalent evidence.

**4. Full episode-faithful socio-technical outcome (low feasibility).**  
“Episode-faithful” and “complete socio-technical” imply the narrative totality: creatures that originate digitally/in-games, evolve autonomously with life-like properties, cross into or tightly couple with the physical world, interact socially with humans at scale, generate emergent impacts, and operate within (or force changes to) legal/ethical/economic systems—without remaining mere components, simulations, or toys.  

No primary or high-quality secondary source documents this having occurred or being on a near-term engineering roadmap for 2026. Barriers include:  
- Technical: open-ended evolution + reliable long-horizon autonomy + safe real-world actuation remain open problems.  
- Embodiment and coupling: soft robotics and digital twins advance but do not yield self-sustaining evolving creature populations with broad agency.  
- Socio-legal: no established frameworks for “digital life” rights, liability, or containment of evolving systems; existing AI governance (EU AI Act and related) focuses on risk tiers for conventional AI, not A-life creatures.  
- Safety/alignment: autonomous evolving systems raise control and unintended-consequence issues that current alignment research has not solved for this domain.  

Media such as Digimon provide the narrative template (franchise materials illustrate the desired outcome fictionally) but supply zero engineering evidence. Progress in generative agents, evolutionary robotics, and AI companions is real yet incremental and component-wise. Expert consensus and roadmaps in A-life and AI do not project the full integrated outcome by mid-2026. Confidence: high on absence of evidence for completion; medium-high on projection that it remains out of reach (AI progress is rapid in narrow domains but A-life open-endedness and full socio-technical embedding have historically lagged).
