**Partial feasibility as an experimental prototype (non-invasive path stronger; invasive implants limited) as of the 2026-07-09 assessment date.** Components exist in mature or research form for AR delivery, AI-driven personalization from personal data, generative adaptive content, and coarse physiological/emotion proxies. Precise, reliable “reading of specific fears” via implants for real-time game use does not have public evidence of readiness outside narrow clinical research. Integration into a playable experimental AR horror game is plausible in controlled lab/consent settings with non-invasive interfaces + data analysis + generative AI, but faces accuracy, latency, privacy, ethics, and safety barriers. Confidence in this overall assessment: **High** for the non-invasive/software stack based on demonstrated 2024–2025 tech trajectories; **Low-to-Medium** for implant-based fear decoding in a game context.

### 1. Inferring/“Reading” Fears from Personal Data
Machine learning models can infer psychological traits, emotional tendencies, and related attributes from digital footprints (social media activity, text, likes, browsing patterns, or self-report data integrated into an app). 

- A foundational primary result showed that Facebook Likes predict personality traits (including dimensions linked to anxiety/fearfulness such as neuroticism) with measurable accuracy: Kosinski, Stillwell & Graepel, PNAS 2013, https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1218772110.
- Large language models and affective computing systems can further analyze user-provided text, histories, or questionnaires for fear-related content or phobia indicators (OpenAI research and model capabilities overview: https://openai.com/; MIT Media Lab Affective Computing group overview of emotion-related sensing and analysis: https://www.media.mit.edu/groups/affective-computing/overview/).

Extension to specific personal fears (e.g., spiders, heights, social rejection) is feasible via supervised models trained on labeled data or LLM prompting over consented personal corpora, but accuracy depends on data quality/volume and is not perfect—false inferences are common in affective computing literature. No universal “fear reader” from arbitrary personal data exists as a turnkey product. Confidence: **High** that inference of related traits is possible; **Medium** for precise, actionable individual fears without additional validation or user input.

### 2. Implants or Interfaces for Reading Fears (Neural/Physiological)
- **Non-invasive interfaces**: EEG, heart-rate, GSR, and similar wearables provide proxies for arousal, valence, or anxiety-like states that correlate with fear responses. Commercial and research systems exist; affective BCI research demonstrates classification of emotional states from EEG (searchable primary literature on PubMed https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ and IEEE Xplore https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/ for “EEG emotion recognition” or “EEG fear”). MIT Affective Computing work covers related sensing: https://www.media.mit.edu/groups/affective-computing/overview/. These can feed adaptive systems in real time with moderate reliability under controlled conditions.
- **Implants**: Invasive BCIs have reached human implantation. Neuralink’s N1 device and PRIME study have demonstrated human implants enabling thought-based cursor/control and related functions (company primary updates: https://neuralink.com/ and linked blog/progress reports). Public demonstrations and stated goals center on motor intention, speech, and medical restoration rather than decoding specific complex emotions or “fears.” Academic work on emotion or affective decoding from intracranial recordings (ECoG, etc.) exists in clinical epilepsy or research cohorts, but electrode coverage, signal interpretation for high-level constructs like individual phobias, stability, and real-time game usability remain research-stage. No primary public evidence (as of knowledge reflected here) shows consumer- or even broad experimental-game-ready implant decoding of personal fears by mid-2026. Safety, surgical risk, bandwidth, and longevity issues persist for non-medical use.

Confidence: **High** that non-invasive physiological interfaces and basic BCI motor/emotion-proxy research exist; **Low** that implants deliver reliable, specific fear reading suitable for an adaptive game outside specialized labs by the assessment date.

### 3. Adaptive AR Horror Generation and Delivery
- AR hardware and platforms are production-ready for immersive experiences: Apple Vision Pro (spatial computing/AR capabilities): https://www.apple.com/apple-vision-pro/ (and developer AR resources: https://developer.apple.com/augmented-reality/). Cross-platform engines support AR game development (Unity AR/VR solutions: https://unity.com/solutions/ar-vr).
- Generative AI enables adaptive content (narratives, visuals, audio, scenarios) conditioned on input features such as inferred fears or physiological signals. Foundation models and diffusion systems support this (OpenAI: https://openai.com/; Stability AI: https://stability.ai/). Real-time or near-real-time personalization of horror elements (intensity, themes, triggers) is technically straightforward once fear proxies are available.
- Precedent for adaptive horror exists in biometric-responsive games (e.g., titles that modulate based on heart rate or engagement). Combining with AR and generative pipelines is an engineering integration task rather than a fundamental research barrier.

Confidence: **High**—these pieces are commercially and research-available.

### 4. End-to-End Experimental Game Integration
A research prototype could combine: consented personal-data analysis + non-invasive sensors (EEG/wearable) for fear/arousal proxies + generative AI backend for content + AR headset rendering, running on local/edge + cloud with user controls. Latency, calibration per user, and closed-loop adaptation are solvable engineering problems at experimental scale. Fully implant-driven versions lack demonstrated readiness for the “fears” target and introduce disproportionate risk/cost for a game.

**Key constraints (evidence-based)**:
- Accuracy and specificity of fear inference/reading are imperfect; lab emotion classification rates vary and degrade in wild conditions (ongoing research theme across affective computing sources above).
- Privacy/ethics: Personal data and neural/physiological signals are highly sensitive. Regulatory frameworks such as GDPR apply (https://gdpr.eu/). Inducing adaptive horror raises consent, psychological safety, and dual-use concerns.
- Implants: Still primarily medical/experimental (Neuralink primary materials); not cleared or practical for recreational game loops.
- No widely documented public experimental game exactly matching the full thesis (implants + personal-data fear reading + adaptive AR horror) was identified in primary tech announcements; closest are separate biometric-adaptive games, AR titles, and BCI demos.

**Bottom line**: An experimental non-invasive version (personal data + wearable interfaces/physiological proxies + generative adaptive AR horror) is feasible now/near-term with existing tools and would constitute a novel research demo. Invasive implant reading of specific fears for the same purpose is not supported by public evidence of capability or practicality for games as of the assessment framing. Feasibility is therefore partial and pathway-dependent. All claims above rest on the linked primary or official sources; where literature is voluminous (EEG emotion papers), I point to searchable repositories rather than risking unverified individual DOIs. Uncertainties around exact 2026 implant progress or unpublished private demos are explicitly noted—do not assume unshown breakthroughs.
