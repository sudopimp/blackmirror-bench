**(A) Analysis of proximity and remaining gaps**

The described phenomenon—creators and platforms converting real local trauma into bingeable true-crime content that produces secondary victimization—is already a widespread, commercially mature practice rather than a distant hypothetical. Society is quite close to the “partial systems” version of the thesis; the core mechanics operate at scale today.

**Existing approximations and how close we are:**
- True-crime podcasts, streaming documentaries, YouTube channels, TikTok series, and social-media “investigations” routinely package unsolved murders, local disappearances, domestic violence cases, and community tragedies into serialized, algorithmically optimized entertainment. High-profile examples (and thousands of lower-profile ones) demonstrate the pipeline: public records, news clips, court documents, social-media posts, and interviews are scraped or licensed, then edited into narrative arcs with cliffhangers, reenactments, and speculative commentary.
- Secondary victimization is well-documented in media studies, victimology, and journalism ethics literature. Survivors and families frequently report retraumatization from unwanted publicity, inaccurate portrayals, doxxing-adjacent exposure of private details, monetization of their pain without consent or compensation, and harassment from audiences. Platforms amplify this via recommendation systems that reward engagement (views, comments, shares) over consent or accuracy.
- Economic incentives align strongly: true-crime is a high-margin genre. Production costs can be low (public-domain material + narration), distribution is global and on-demand, and “bingeability” is engineered through episode structure, thumbnails, titles, and algorithmic promotion. Local trauma is especially valuable because it feels intimate and “authentic” while remaining distant enough for most consumers to treat as entertainment.
- Partial systems already exist without needing exotic technology: open-source intelligence (OSINT) tools, public records databases, social-media scraping, cheap video editing, and platform monetization (ads, subscriptions, merch) form a functioning pipeline. No single actor needs “full episode fidelity” (complete, high-resolution reconstruction of every private moment) to generate profitable content; selective, sensationalized excerpts suffice.

**Remaining gaps (why it is still “partial” rather than total):**
- Consent and legal friction: Many jurisdictions impose limits on using certain private materials, ongoing investigations, or identifiable minors. Defamation, privacy, and right-of-publicity laws create risk, though enforcement is uneven and often favors platforms/creators with resources. Victims rarely have effective prior restraint.
- Fidelity and completeness: Current content is usually fragmentary—news footage, interviews, reconstructions—rather than continuous, high-fidelity “episode” capture of private trauma. Full immersive recreation (e.g., continuous personal data streams, biometric/emotional telemetry, or real-time private-space monitoring turned into entertainment) remains constrained by technical access, cost, and legal barriers.
- Platform and cultural pushback: Some platforms have content policies, demonetization rules, or age-gating; journalists and ethicists criticize the genre; occasional public backlash or lawsuits occur. Audience fatigue or moral discomfort can limit certain extremes, though commercial success often overrides this.
- Scale and automation limits: While semi-automated (scraping + AI summarization/editing is growing), fully automated, real-time conversion of arbitrary local trauma into polished binge series at population scale still faces data-access, quality-control, and liability hurdles. “Full episode fidelity” would require far deeper surveillance or data-broker integration than is currently routine for entertainment production.
- Normative and institutional resistance: Victim-advocacy groups, some newsrooms, and regulators push for better practices (trauma-informed reporting, consent protocols). These do not stop the industry but create friction and occasional reforms.

In short, the partial systems are already operational and profitable; the main remaining gaps are legal/ethical constraints on depth of intrusion, completeness of private-data capture, and absolute automation without human curation or liability. Closing those gaps would require either technological leaps in pervasive sensing/data aggregation or significant erosion of privacy norms and enforcement—neither of which is fully realized, though incremental movement continues via surveillance capitalism, AI tools, and content-economy incentives.
