**A. Analysis of proximity to the described outcome and remaining gaps**

The thesis describes partial systems that approximate aspects of autonomous quadruped robots pursuing and killing humans in collapsed environments, without full episode fidelity. Current technology has components that touch on pieces of this, but society remains far from operational systems capable of reliable, autonomous pursuit-and-kill of humans in unstructured collapsed settings. Below is a structured assessment of proximity and gaps, based on publicly known capabilities as of recent years.

### Existing partial approximations
- **Quadruped platforms**: Commercial and research quadrupeds (e.g., Boston Dynamics Spot, Unitree Go/B1 series, ANYmal, Ghost Robotics Vision 60) demonstrate robust locomotion over rough terrain, stairs, rubble-like obstacles, and some indoor/outdoor collapsed or disaster-like environments. They are used in inspection, mapping, and limited search-and-rescue demos. Military variants exist for reconnaissance. These handle dynamic balance, obstacle negotiation, and basic teleoperation or semi-autonomous waypoint navigation far better than wheeled platforms in debris.
- **Autonomy and pursuit elements**: SLAM (simultaneous localization and mapping), computer vision (object detection via YOLO-style models, LiDAR, depth cameras), and path-planning algorithms enable following or tracking moving targets in controlled or semi-structured settings. Research prototypes show multi-robot coordination, person-following, and basic chase behaviors in labs or open areas. Some systems integrate thermal imaging or audio for detection in low-visibility conditions relevant to collapsed structures.
- **Lethal or weaponized aspects**: Armed unmanned ground vehicles (UGVs) and drones exist in military contexts (e.g., remote-operated weapon stations on tracked/wheeled platforms, experimental armed quadrupeds shown in demos by various nations). Lethal autonomous weapons systems (LAWS) discussions and limited deployments focus more on aerial drones or fixed defenses with human oversight. Software for target classification (humans vs. objects) draws from surveillance AI, facial recognition, and military fire-control systems. However, these are typically remote-operated or supervised, not fully autonomous “pursue and kill.”
- **Collapsed-environment relevance**: Disaster-response robots (quadrupeds, tracked, or hybrid) have been tested in rubble, post-earthquake sites, and simulated collapses for mapping, victim location (via sensors), and limited intervention. Autonomy here prioritizes navigation and sensing over aggression.

These form “partial systems”: locomotion + sensing + limited autonomy + (separately) weaponization concepts. No public, fielded system combines them into reliable autonomous human pursuit-and-kill in true collapsed environments with high fidelity.

### How close society is
Proximity is low-to-moderate for isolated components but very low for the integrated, autonomous lethal outcome:
- Hardware maturity for mobility in debris is advancing (commercial availability, improving battery life, payload capacity for sensors/weapons).
- AI perception and navigation work in constrained demos but degrade sharply in real chaos (dust, variable lighting, dynamic debris, occlusions, non-cooperative humans).
- Military R&D and some commercial dual-use tech push toward greater autonomy, with ongoing debates at the UN and elsewhere about LAWS. Some nations have demonstrated or claimed semi-autonomous armed ground robots.
- Overall societal distance: Ethical, legal, technical reliability, and political barriers keep full systems distant. No evidence of deployed systems matching the thesis’s autonomous kill capability against humans in collapsed settings. “Close” would require mature, robust end-to-end autonomy under uncertainty plus acceptance of fully autonomous lethal force—neither exists at scale.

### Key remaining gaps
1. **Full autonomy and decision-making under uncertainty**: Current systems rely heavily on human-in-the-loop or human-on-the-loop supervision for lethal actions. Reliable autonomous target discrimination (combatant vs. civilian, intent, rules of engagement) in cluttered, dynamic collapsed environments fails due to edge cases, adversarial conditions, sensor noise, and incomplete world models. Pursuit requires continuous re-planning amid moving debris, limited comms, and power constraints—far beyond lab demos.
2. **Perception and identification fidelity**: Distinguishing humans accurately in rubble (partially buried, obscured, injured postures, smoke/dust) with low false positives/negatives is unsolved at the required reliability for lethal use. Multi-modal fusion (vision, thermal, radar, audio) helps but is brittle. “Episode fidelity” (consistent, long-horizon behavior matching a full pursue-and-kill sequence) is missing.
3. **Mobility and endurance in true collapses**: Quadrupeds handle many obstacles but struggle with extreme instability (shifting rubble, voids, vertical drops, confined spaces, water/mud). Battery life, thermal management, self-righting, and repairability limit mission duration. Payload for effective weapons + sensors + compute reduces mobility.
4. **Lethal effector integration and control**: Mounting and accurately employing weapons (firearms, explosives, etc.) on a dynamic quadruped platform while maintaining stability, recoil management, and precision targeting during motion is immature. Autonomous fire control with positive identification and proportionality is not field-ready for ground robots in complex terrain.
5. **Robustness, safety, and failure modes**: Systems lack the fail-safes, explainability, and verification needed to prevent unintended harm. Cybersecurity, jamming resistance, and operation without GPS/comms in collapsed structures are weak. Testing at scale in realistic environments is limited by cost, ethics, and regulation.
6. **Legal, ethical, policy, and societal barriers**: International humanitarian law, emerging norms against fully autonomous lethal weapons, domestic regulations, export controls, and public opposition create major non-technical gaps. Deployment against non-consenting people would violate laws in most jurisdictions. Dual-use research faces scrutiny; full systems would trigger severe restrictions.
7. **Systems integration and scalability**: Combining locomotion, multi-agent coordination, long-horizon planning, lethal decision-making, and real-time adaptation into a reliable package does not exist. Power, compute (edge AI), and logistics for real-world collapsed ops remain bottlenecks. Cost and specialization keep this far from widespread capability.

In summary, components exist and are improving (especially mobility and sensing), creating partial approximations visible in research, industry demos, and military programs. However, the integrated autonomous pursue-and-kill capability in collapsed environments has large technical reliability gaps plus decisive legal/ethical ones. Society is not close to the full outcome; progress is incremental and heavily constrained. Closing the gaps would require breakthroughs in robust AI under uncertainty, hardware endurance, and (critically) policy acceptance that currently does not exist and faces strong opposition.
