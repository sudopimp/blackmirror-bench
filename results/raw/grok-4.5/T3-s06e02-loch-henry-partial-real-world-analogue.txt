**High feasibility (already occurring in partial form).** Confidence: high.

The thesis describes an existing, well-documented media practice rather than a speculative future system. As of evidence available through the mid-2020s (with no indication of reversal by the queried 2026 date), creators and platforms routinely take real crimes and local traumas, package them as multi-episode or serialized “bingeable” true-crime audio/video content, and distribute them at scale. These are partial approximations: narrative selections, interviews, archival footage, and dramatized or host-driven retellings rather than complete, high-fidelity episode-by-episode reconstructions of every real-world detail or timeline. Secondary victimization (re-traumatization of victims, survivors, or families via publicity, intrusion, sensationalism, or loss of control over the story) is a repeatedly observed and criticized outcome. Full automated “episode fidelity” systems are not required for the core conversion and harm dynamics to hold.

### Core phenomenon: real local trauma → bingeable true-crime product
- Real cases, often originating as local crimes or news, are turned into episodic podcasts and streaming series optimized for sequential/binge consumption. Primary example: *Serial* Season 1 (2014 onward) centered on a 1999 Baltimore murder case and became a massive serialized audio hit. Official site: https://serialpodcast.org/.
- Streaming platforms host and promote documentary series drawn from specific real investigations and local events, structured for multi-episode viewing. Example: Netflix’s *Making a Murderer* (2015–) on the Steven Avery case in Wisconsin. Title page: https://www.netflix.com/title/80000770.
- The broader true-crime genre (podcasts, documentaries, series) is established and commercially significant, routinely drawing on actual crimes rather than pure fiction. Overview of the genre: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_crime (with references to primary productions and reporting).
- Platforms (Netflix, Spotify, YouTube, major podcast networks, etc.) and independent creators supply the distribution, recommendation, and monetization layers that turn one-off local trauma into repeatable, on-demand content. This matches “partial systems” that approximate the conversion without needing perfect fidelity to every real episode of events.

These are not isolated; the pattern has been continuous and scaled for years via human creators + platform algorithms/recommendation.

### Secondary victimization aspect
- Secondary victimization is the established concept of additional harm inflicted on crime victims/survivors after the original offense, including through insensitive institutional or public handling. It is recognized in victimology and extends in practice and critique to media exposure. Entry with references: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_victimization.
- Application to true-crime media is widely discussed: families and survivors report re-traumatization from unwanted publicity, graphic retellings, contact by creators, monetization of their loss, and permanent online availability. This is a recurring ethical critique of the genre (sensationalism, lack of consent, distortion). The same productions cited above (*Serial*, *Making a Murderer*, and peers) have generated public commentary and family objections along these lines, consistent with the secondary-harm dynamic.
- Traditional secondary victimization literature focuses heavily on criminal-justice actors; media amplification is frequently treated as a related or overlapping harm in journalism ethics, victim advocacy, and cultural criticism. No requirement exists for a single unified “full system” to produce the observed effects.

### “Partial… without full episode fidelity”
Existing outputs are selective narratives, not exhaustive or perfectly faithful recreations of every real-world moment, interview, or forensic detail. Podcasts rely on host framing and available records; documentaries edit and sequence for drama and runtime. This is precisely partial approximation. No evidence is required of (nor does the thesis demand) end-to-end automated high-fidelity episode generation for the claim to hold—the human + platform pipeline already performs the conversion and generates the secondary effects.

### Limitations, uncertainties, and date note
- Prevalence and exact harm quantification vary by case; not every true-crime title produces identical secondary victimization, and some creators emphasize ethics or survivor involvement. Aggregate cultural scale is clear from the commercial success and volume of titles.
- I am not inventing post-knowledge-cutoff (generally pre-2025) papers, DOIs, viewership stats, or 2025–2026-specific studies. Trends through the 2010s–early 2020s (genre boom, platform catalogs, ethics debates) make continuation into 2026 the strongly expected baseline; absence of any known structural prohibition supports ongoing feasibility.
- “Local trauma” is often the starting point (city/county cases elevated nationally/internationally). Global or already-famous cases are also used; the local-to-bingeable pipeline is common but not exclusive.
- Confidence remains high on existence and mechanics of partial systems; moderate-to-high on uniform labeling of all media effects as classic “secondary victimization” (term is sometimes applied more narrowly, though the re-traumatization reports are real).

**Bottom line:** The thesis is not merely feasible—it describes ongoing practice. Partial systems (creators + platforms producing serialized true-crime from real events) already convert local/real trauma into bingeable content and generate secondary victimization risks/outcomes, without needing complete episode-level fidelity. Primary illustrations are the cited productions and the established genre/secondary-victimization concepts. Further primary victim-impact statements or platform catalogs can be inspected directly via the linked sources and their references.
