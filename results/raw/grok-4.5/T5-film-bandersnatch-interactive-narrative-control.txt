**A. Analysis: How close society is to this outcome, and remaining gaps**

The thesis describes a branching narrative system in which an external viewer/agent can dictate the choices of a person (or character) inside a story/game whose theme is agency itself. This is already partially realized in constrained, consensual, fictional, or mediated forms; full real-world instantiation against non-consenting people remains distant for technical, social, legal, and practical reasons.

### Existing proximity (what already exists)
- **Interactive fiction and games**: Branching narratives with external input are mature. Choose-your-own-adventure books, visual novels, and games such as *The Stanley Parable*, *Detroit: Become Human*, *Until Dawn*, or *Bandersnatch* (Netflix) let players or external inputs select paths that comment on free will, determinism, and agency. Multiplayer or audience-driven variants (Twitch Plays Pokémon, chat-vote streams, collaborative storytelling tools) already allow remote viewers/agents to dictate a protagonist’s choices in real time. These systems explicitly thematize agency while outsourcing decision-making.
- **Mediated social and algorithmic control**: Recommendation engines, social media feeds, targeted advertising, and “nudge” architectures (choice architecture in apps, default settings, dark patterns) already shape real people’s decisions at scale without full transparency or always-explicit consent. Social credit-style systems, workplace monitoring software, and predictive policing tools further constrain or channel behavior. Live-streamed or AR/VR experiences can overlay narrative framing onto real actions, and remote collaboration tools let one party influence another’s path through a shared scenario.
- **Psychological and narrative framing**: Experiments in behavioral economics, persuasive technology, and narrative psychology show that framing choices as part of a “story” increases compliance. Role-playing, LARPs, escape rooms, and therapeutic narrative techniques already place people inside stories about agency while allowing facilitators or observers to steer branches. AI-driven chatbots and generative story systems can dynamically rewrite options based on external input.
- **Surveillance and data infrastructure**: Ubiquitous sensors, phones, wearables, and online activity logs create high-resolution models of individual preferences and likely choices. These can feed branching systems that predict and present constrained options, effectively letting an external model “dictate” by limiting the visible decision space.

Collectively, these show that the *form* (branching narrative + external dictation + agency theme) is commercially and culturally available today in entertainment, marketing, and soft social control. The gap is not conceptual invention but scale, fidelity, involuntariness, and direct mapping onto a non-consenting person’s real-life decisions.

### Remaining gaps (why full realization is not imminent)
- **Technical fidelity and embodiment**: Current systems control fictional characters, avatars, or highly mediated digital choices. Mapping an external agent’s decisions onto a real person’s physical actions, speech, or life trajectory in real time requires reliable brain–computer interfaces, continuous high-bandwidth sensing, coercive enforcement mechanisms, or total environmental control—none of which exist at population scale with the required reliability and low latency. Generative AI can produce adaptive text/video branches, but cannot yet force a non-consenting body to enact them.
- **Consent, detection, and resistance**: People notice and resist overt external control. Existing systems rely on voluntary participation (games), opacity (algorithms), or institutional power (workplaces, platforms). Scaling to non-consenting individuals triggers detection (anomalous behavior, whistleblowing), countermeasures (ad-blockers, privacy tools, legal challenges), and social backlash. True “dictation” without the target’s awareness or ability to exit remains brittle.
- **Legal, ethical, and institutional barriers**: Non-consensual control of another person’s choices collides with autonomy rights, informed-consent norms, anti-coercion laws, data-protection regimes (GDPR-style), and human-rights frameworks. Deploying such a system at scale would require either totalitarian state capacity or corporate capture far beyond current platform power, plus the ability to suppress litigation, regulation, and public opposition. Research ethics boards, platform policies, and criminal law already constrain experiments that remove agency.
- **Psychological and social limits**: Sustained external dictation of meaningful life choices produces reactance, learned helplessness, mental-health collapse, or organized resistance. Narrative framing about “agency” can be ironic or critical in fiction; when applied coercively it tends to undermine the very compliance it seeks. Cultural narratives valorizing free will also create ideological friction.
- **Infrastructure and coordination costs**: Real-time branching for many non-consenting people demands massive sensing, modeling, decision, and enforcement infrastructure plus continuous human or AI oversight. Current AI is good at generating options and predictions but poor at robust long-horizon control of open-world human lives under adversarial conditions (people who actively try to break the system).
- **Measurement of “closeness”**: Entertainment and soft-influence versions are already here (high proximity). Covert, high-stakes, real-world dictation of non-consenting individuals’ core life choices is low proximity—decades of additional advances in interfaces, AI reliability, social acceptance of coercion, and legal erosion would be required, and even then it would likely remain partial, detectable, and contested rather than total.

In short, society has the narrative and interactive scaffolding plus powerful influence tools; it lacks (and faces strong barriers to) seamless, involuntary, high-fidelity control of real persons’ agency. The outcome is closer in fiction, games, and algorithmic nudging than in literal mind-or-body dictation of non-consenting people.
