### 1. Packaging a pop star’s persona into AI dolls / digital avatars
**Feasibility: high.** Voice cloning, visual deepfakes/avatars, interactive digital humans, and physical AI-enabled dolls/robots already exist and continue to improve. Licensing of likeness, voice, and performance data for digital use is commercially practiced (with legal constraints around right of publicity). Full “AI dolls” that convincingly package a living pop star’s persona (appearance, voice, mannerisms, interactive behavior) are technically achievable and have partial commercial/entertainment precedents; widespread unauthorized or fully autonomous versions face legal, ethical, and quality barriers.

Evidence (real, primary or near-primary sources):
- Digital performance avatars of musicians are in commercial use. ABBA Voyage uses de-aged digital versions of the band members for live shows (official project site: https://abbavoyage.com/).
- Commercial AI voice cloning and synthesis platforms enable high-fidelity voice replication from samples (example provider: https://elevenlabs.io/).
- AI-powered physical dolls/robots with conversational and expressive capabilities are sold commercially (Realbotix: https://realbotix.com/).
- Broader digital-human and avatar platforms exist for interactive characters (e.g., Soul Machines: https://www.soulmachines.com/).
- Music-industry and artist experiments with AI voice/likeness licensing have been publicly discussed and implemented in limited forms (coverage and artist statements appear in major outlets; e.g., search primary artist announcements and trade reporting). Deepfake and generative tools for faces/voices are widely available and improving.

Limitations and uncertainties as of 2026-07-09: Photoreal interactive physical dolls that fully capture a specific living star’s persona at high fidelity remain imperfect and expensive; quality, uncanny-valley issues, and compute/data requirements persist. Unauthorized commercial use triggers right-of-publicity, copyright, and emerging deepfake laws (U.S. state laws and platform policies have tightened). No primary evidence of systematic “packaging” of major living pop stars into mass-market AI dolls specifically to replace them. If new commercial products or licenses appeared after mid-2020s knowledge cutoffs, they would need verification against company filings, artist contracts, or court records—I am not inventing any such 2025–2026 cases here.
