### 1. Inferring traits, anxieties, or fear-related signals from personal/digital data (partial approximation: yes)
Machine learning on digital footprints (likes, posts, behavior) can predict private psychological traits and attributes with above-chance accuracy. This approximates “reading fears from personal data” at a coarse, probabilistic level (personality, interests, mental-health signals) rather than precise, validated phobia inventories or real-time fear content.

- Primary evidence: Kosinski, Stillwell & Graepel (2013) showed Facebook Likes predict sensitive attributes including personality dimensions. https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1218772110 (High confidence in paper and findings as published).
- Related mental-health signal detection from social data exists in the literature (depression, anxiety markers), supporting extension to fear-related constructs, though specificity for discrete fears (e.g., “spiders vs. heights”) is weaker and error-prone without ground-truth labels. Broader psychometrics-from-data work builds on the above. Confidence in existence of the approach: High; in precise “fear reading” ready for games: Low-Medium.

Commercial recommendation and ad systems routinely personalize from personal data; academic extensions to affective or clinical signals are documented. Full mapping to actionable “fears” for horror generation remains approximate and ethically fraught (consent, accuracy, dual-use).

### 2. Interfaces (and experimental implants) for reading emotional/fear-related states (partial: yes for non-invasive; limited for implants)
Consumer and research interfaces detect arousal, valence, stress, or emotion proxies via EEG, heart-rate/HRV, GSR, facial coding, etc. These support adaptive responses to fear-like states. Specific semantic “fears” (content of what is feared) are not directly “read” with high fidelity from signals alone; they require calibration, context, or fusion with other data. Implants remain experimental and not game-ready for this purpose.

- Consumer EEG emotion/cognitive-state systems: Emotiv platforms market detection of emotional states from EEG. https://www.emotiv.com/ (High confidence in product existence and claims; scientific accuracy of consumer emotion labels is debated and lower than lab settings).
- Biofeedback horror game using fear/arousal signals to adapt intensity: *Nevermind* (psychological horror) monitors physiological or facial indicators of fear/stress and modulates the experience. https://store.steampowered.com/app/388110/Nevermind/ (High confidence; primary storefront and documented mechanic).
- Experimental high-bandwidth BCI implants: Neuralink develops implantable BCIs; human implants demonstrated for neural signal decoding (primarily motor/cursor intent as of mid-2020s announcements). https://neuralink.com/ (High confidence in company, goals, and early human work). No public evidence of fear-content decoding or game integration; medical/research focus, regulatory and safety barriers remain. By mid-2026 trajectory, more subjects and bandwidth expected, but consumer “fear-reading implant for AR horror” is not supported. Confidence in implant readiness for the thesis use-case: Low.
- Wearable proxies (HRV/stress): Widely available (e.g., Apple Watch health features). https://www.apple.com/apple-watch/ (and related healthcare pages). Useful for arousal, not specific fears.

Fusion of personal-data priors + real-time interface is conceptually straightforward in research (affective computing) but not evidenced as a polished game loop with implants.

### 3. Adaptive horror generation and AR delivery (partial: yes)
Procedural/AI-director systems adapt horror pacing/threat; AR platforms and some horror titles exist; generative models can personalize text/narrative horror from prompts or profiles. Full real-time, high-fidelity adaptive AR horror *driven by inferred personal fears via implant* is not evidenced as a complete system.

- Adaptive AI in horror: *Alien: Isolation* uses an AI director that dynamically adjusts xenomorph behavior and tension. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alien:_Isolation (and official materials; High confidence in the adaptive design).
- AR platforms enabling overlay experiences: Apple ARKit. https://developer.apple.com/augmented-reality/ (High confidence). Meta Quest passthrough AR and related XR hardware support mixed-reality horror-style content.
- AR horror title example: *Five Nights at Freddy’s AR: Special Delivery*. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Nights_at_Freddy%27s_AR:_Special_Delivery (High confidence in existence).
- Generative personalization: Large language/multimodal models can produce customized horror narratives or descriptions from user data/prompts (OpenAI systems). https://openai.com/ (High confidence in capability for text/image; real-time 3D AR asset generation and coherent episode fidelity remain harder and more limited).
- VR/AR exposure and affective adaptation research exists for phobias/anxiety (therapeutic rather than entertainment horror), providing further partial precedent, though primary clinical sources emphasize controlled protocols over games.

No primary source documents a single experimental game that closes the full loop (personal-data fear model + implant/interface read + generative adaptive AR horror episode) at high fidelity. Approximations combine the above pieces in research demos, biofeedback titles, or personalized generative tools.

### 4. Overall integration, “experimental game,” and 2026 outlook
- **Partial systems without full episode fidelity**: Strongly supported. Examples include bio-adaptive horror (*Nevermind*), psychometrics-from-data (Kosinski et al.), consumer emotion interfaces (Emotiv), experimental implants (Neuralink), adaptive game AI, AR horror titles, and generative models. These approximate aspects (data-driven personalization, fear/arousal sensing, adaptive tension, AR delivery, generative content). Confidence: High.
- **Full thesis system**: Not evidenced. Barriers include: (a) low specificity/reliability of “fear” inference (data or neural) without extensive per-user labeling; (b) implants not approved/available for consumer entertainment or precise affective content decoding; (c) real-time generative AR (coherent, low-latency, high-quality horror scenes reacting to signals) still immature vs. pre-authored or simpler procedural; (d) privacy, consent, safety, and ethics (especially implants + personal data + horror) would constrain any experiment; (e) “episode fidelity” (sustained, narratively coherent, effective adaptive horror) requires integration and playtesting far beyond component demos. Confidence in non-existence of full system as of knowledge base: High.
- **2026-07-09 projection**: Incremental advances likely (more BCI subjects/bandwidth per Neuralink trajectory, better generative XR tools, refined affective models, wider AR hardware). Partial approximations will be stronger; a tightly integrated experimental prototype remains plausible only in constrained lab settings, not as a robust game. No basis to claim full fidelity. Confidence in projection: Medium (trend extrapolation only; no invented 2025–2026 results).

**Key caveats (evidence-based)**: Inference is correlational and error-prone; emotion detection ≠ reading specific fears; implants carry surgical/medical risk and are not general interfaces; adaptive horror can increase intensity via arousal but personalization quality varies; AR has field-of-view, tracking, and comfort limits. Ethical/regulatory hurdles (data protection, BCI oversight, vulnerable users in horror contexts) are material. Primary sources above do not describe or endorse a combined harmful or non-consensual system.
