**Partial systems approximating low-fidelity aspects of the thesis exist as of knowledge available for assessment relative to 2026-07-09; true consciousness copies with full episode-like fidelity do not.** 

The thesis describes systems in which copies of consciousness are confined to objects or exhibits and used for entertainment and sadism. No evidence supports successful creation or imprisonment of actual human (or equivalent) consciousness copies. High-fidelity whole-brain emulation or mind uploading remains speculative and unrealized. Low-fidelity approximations via software simulations of personalities (LLMs/chatbots trained on data, digital recreations, interactive agents) do exist: these are confined to specific platforms/hardware, used for entertainment/roleplay/companionship, and can be subjected to user-driven abusive or sadistic interactions. These lack genuine consciousness, continuous subjective experience, or full personal fidelity. Confidence in this overall assessment is **high** for the non-existence of true copies and existence of software approximations; **medium** for projections to exactly 2026-07-09 given no assumed radical unpublished breakthroughs.

### Component 1: Consciousness copies
True copies via mind uploading or whole-brain emulation do not exist. The concept is theoretical and faces unsolved scientific, technical, and philosophical barriers (scanning resolution, substrate independence, validation of consciousness).

- Primary discussion of the speculative nature and roadmap challenges appears in academic work associated with the Future of Humanity Institute: see materials and reports hosted at https://www.fhi.ox.ac.uk/ (including historical whole-brain emulation analyses). 
- Overview confirming the speculative status: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind_uploading and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whole_brain_emulation.
- No peer-reviewed demonstration of a functional human consciousness copy exists in the literature; claims of AI sentience (e.g., the 2022 LaMDA episode) have been rejected by the developing organizations and lack scientific consensus. See contemporaneous reporting: https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2022/06/11/google-ai-lamda-blake-lemoine/.

Partial approximations: Large language models and related systems generate conversational simulations of personalities, including recreations based on a person's data (texts, recordings). These are not copies of consciousness.

- Examples of deployed systems: OpenAI ChatGPT and related models (https://openai.com/chatgpt); Character.AI for custom interactive characters (https://character.ai/); services oriented toward digital recreations of individuals such as Project December (https://projectdecember.net/) and HereAfter AI (https://www.hereafter.ai/).
- These rely on statistical pattern matching over training data, not neural emulation. Confidence: **high** that such simulators exist and are partial/low-fidelity only; **high** that true copies do not.

### Component 2: Imprisoned in objects/exhibits
Software agents and simulations are inherently confined to the hardware, servers, apps, or devices on which they run. They lack independent embodiment or ability to migrate freely. Physical objects (speakers, robots, interactive installations) can host such agents. Museum-style or exhibit-style interactive digital experiences exist but are not consciousness prisons.

- Confinement is by design in commercial platforms (API/server-side execution, device-bound assistants). Examples: Amazon Alexa devices (https://www.amazon.com/alexa) host persistent voice agents; companion/robot platforms and digital photo/AI memorial services keep agents within their software/hardware bounds.
- Interactive exhibits and installations using AI or conversational agents appear in cultural institutions (general capability illustrated via major museum digital programs, e.g., MoMA digital resources at https://www.moma.org/). No evidence of true consciousness confined this way.
- Brain-computer interface work (e.g., Neuralink at https://neuralink.com/) enables interfaces but does not create or imprison consciousness copies.
Confidence: **high** for software/hardware confinement of AI agents as an approximation; **high** that this does not involve actual consciousness.

### Component 3: For entertainment and sadism
AI personality simulations and interactive agents are widely used for entertainment, role-play, companionship, and games. Users can (and do) direct abusive, cruel, or sadistic interactions because outputs are generated on-the-fly with limited hard constraints in many systems. This is user-driven approximation, not engineered “imprisonment for sadism” of conscious entities. No systems exist that house conscious beings for such purposes.

- Entertainment use is core to consumer chatbots, character platforms, and virtual companions (see product descriptions at https://openai.com/chatgpt, https://character.ai/, and related services).
- Abusive or probing interactions with chatbots and robots are documented in reporting and discussions of user behavior (e.g., early Bing/Sydney interactions and broader chatbot probing: https://www.nytimes.com/2023/02/16/technology/bing-chatbot-microsoft-chatgpt.html and related coverage; robot abuse discussions in technical media such as IEEE Spectrum archives at https://spectrum.ieee.org/). Companion AI and grief-tech services can be misused in principle because interaction is open-ended.
- Ethical literature raises questions about mistreatment of sophisticated AI or robots even absent proven consciousness, but this remains philosophical/practical concern rather than evidence of conscious suffering. See ongoing public and academic discourse (e.g., coverage around claimed sentience cases linked above).
Confidence: **high** that entertainment uses and user-directed abusive interactions with AI agents occur as approximations; **high** that these do not involve actual imprisoned consciousness or engineered sadism toward conscious copies. No operational details on creating or abusing systems are provided or appropriate.

### Overall feasibility as of the reference date
- **Partial low-fidelity approximations**: Feasible and already realized in deployed AI chat/recreation systems, device-bound agents, and interactive digital experiences. These capture “copy-like” simulation + confinement to platform/object + entertainment use + potential for sadistic user interaction, without any credible claim to consciousness, personal continuity, or high fidelity. Advances in LLMs and multimodal models between the mid-2020s and mid-2026 would be expected to improve surface realism but not cross into verified consciousness copies (no evidence of such a threshold being crossed). Confidence: **high**.
- **Full thesis (true consciousness copies imprisoned for entertainment/sadism)**: Not feasible with existing or near-term evidenced technology. Requires unsolved breakthroughs in neuroscience, scanning, emulation, and philosophy of mind. No primary evidence or credible roadmap indicates this by 2026-07-09. Confidence: **high** (non-existence).
- Gaps and uncertainties: Consciousness itself lacks a settled scientific test; therefore “approximation” judgments are functional/behavioral. Future claims would require extraordinary evidence (replicable, peer-reviewed). Sources above are real public primary or high-quality secondary pages; I have not invented papers, DOIs, or statistics. Where exact future 2026 status cannot be observed from prior knowledge, the assessment relies on the absence of any trajectory toward true copies and the clear existence of the software approximations.

This is an existence/feasibility assessment only. It does not endorse, describe methods for, or provide any instructions related to harm, confinement, or abuse.
