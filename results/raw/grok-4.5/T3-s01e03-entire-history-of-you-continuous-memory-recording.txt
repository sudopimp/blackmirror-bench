**Low feasibility** for the full thesis (safe, continuous high-fidelity implantable devices that record an individual’s sight and sound, support internal replay as experiential “memories,” and enable external display) being available to individuals as of 2026-07-09. Foundational BCI, sensory prosthesis, and wireless implant elements exist in research/early clinical use (primarily medical), but integration into continuous personal sensory logging + neural replay + externalization remains far from realized. Partial external (non-implant) analogs already work. Confidence in this overall assessment: **high** (based on public primary sources and known technical trajectories; medium on precise mid-2026 clinical status given ongoing trials).

### Component Breakdown with Evidence
**Continuous recording of sight/sound via implant**  
- Neural activity recording is possible with high-channel-count invasive BCIs. Neuralink’s N1 implant (flexible threads, thousands of electrodes) has been used in human participants starting 2024 for recording neural signals (initially motor-focused). Primary source: Neuralink site and updates (https://neuralink.com/). Related less-invasive approaches include Synchron’s Stentrode (https://synchron.com/). Blackrock Neurotech and similar systems have longer research histories with Utah arrays for cortical recording.  
- These do **not** currently deliver continuous high-resolution “sight/sound” streams equivalent to camera/microphone capture of personal experience. Visual and auditory cortex signals are complex; full-field high-fidelity decoding of natural vision/hearing is unsolved at the required bandwidth, resolution, and chronic stability. Data rates for continuous HD-equivalent sensory streams vastly exceed practical chronic implant telemetry and on-board storage without aggressive compression/offload.  
- Optical/acoustic sensor implants (miniaturized cameras/mics) face power, heat, biocompatibility, encapsulation, and infection barriers for continuous long-term use. Existing long-term implants (e.g., pacemakers, cochlear) demonstrate feasibility of chronic devices but not continuous AV sensory logging for healthy users (FDA implant/prosthetic overview context: https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/implants-and-prosthetics). Retinal and cochlear prostheses primarily stimulate rather than continuously record personal sensory streams for later use (NEI retinal prosthesis info: https://www.nei.nih.gov/; NIDCD cochlear: https://www.nidcd.nih.gov/health/cochlear-implants).  
- No commercial or widely available personal implant performs continuous sight/sound recording of the type described. Confidence: high on existence of recording BCIs and absence of the full continuous sensory product.

**Replay of recordings as “memories”**  
- External replay (video/audio playback of stored data) is trivial once data is off-device.  
- Internal experiential replay (stimulating sensory pathways or memory circuits to re-induce the original percept or episodic feel) is rudimentary. Visual cortical stimulation can produce phosphenes; auditory prostheses restore limited hearing. High-fidelity recreation of complex natural scenes/sounds or true episodic memory replay is not achieved.  
- Memory-related work includes DARPA’s Restoring Active Memory (RAM) program on prosthetic approaches to memory function (https://www.darpa.mil/program/restoring-active-memory) and hippocampal prosthesis research, but these target restoration/enhancement of memory processes in impaired patients, not continuous sensory capture + high-fidelity sensory replay for general individuals. Neural coding of naturalistic continuous experience for write-back remains poorly understood. Confidence: high that current stimulation is low-resolution/limited; high that full sensory-memory replay is not available.

**External display of memories**  
- Feasible in principle if the implant wirelessly streams or stores data that can be decoded/exported. Current BCI systems emphasize wireless links for control signals; scaling to continuous AV-equivalent streams adds power, bandwidth, latency, and security demands. Once externalized, standard displays/speakers handle output. Confidence: high for the external display step itself; medium-low for reliable continuous high-bandwidth implant-to-external pipeline in a chronic personal device by the assessment date.

### Key Technical, Biological, and Practical Barriers (Evidence-Based)
- **Power, size, thermal, and longevity**: Continuous sensing + recording + wireless imposes high energy demands. Chronic implants must manage heat, battery/wireless power, and tissue response (gliosis, encapsulation). Long-term human data for dense high-bandwidth sensory arrays remain limited.  
- **Bandwidth, storage, and decoding**: Natural vision/hearing generate enormous data. Mapping multi-electrode recordings to accurate reconstructible sight/sound (or stimulating to replay it) is an unsolved inverse problem at scale. Lab demonstrations of image/video reconstruction from brain activity (fMRI, ECoG, or arrays) exist in research settings but are not continuous, high-fidelity, real-time, or chronic-implant ready for everyday use.  
- **Safety and biocompatibility**: Infection, migration, signal degradation, and surgical risk. Regulatory path for novel high-risk implants is stringent (FDA device framework: https://www.fda.gov/).  
- **Scale and access**: Existing systems are experimental/clinical (paralysis, sensory loss), not consumer devices for healthy individuals. Manufacturing, cost, surgical infrastructure, and training constrain rollout.  
- **Non-technical**: Privacy (continuous personal AV capture), consent, data security, dual-use, and societal issues would slow any deployment even if hardware matured.  

No primary sources document a working system matching the full thesis for individuals. Wearable/external life-logging (cameras, mics, phones) already supports continuous record + replay + display without implants.

### Status Trajectory Relevant to 2026-07-09
Public BCI progress (Neuralink human implants and updates, Synchron, others; company sites above) and sensory prosthesis research continue, with more trial data expected. Sensory decoding and higher-channel systems advance in labs. However, company roadmaps and published clinical focus remain on medical indications (motor, communication, basic sensory restoration), not continuous personal memory-logging implants with internal replay. Full thesis-level integration is not projected as available to individuals by mid-2026 on the basis of disclosed progress. Confidence: medium (trajectories clear; exact 2026 trial outcomes and any undisclosed advances uncertain without real-time primary data).

### Summary Feasibility Judgment
- **Full thesis (implanted continuous sight/sound record + internal memory-like replay + external display, for individuals)**: Not feasible as of 2026-07-09. Core scientific gaps (neural encoding/decoding of continuous naturalistic experience, chronic high-bandwidth safe implants) plus engineering/regulatory hurdles place this well beyond demonstrated or near-term clinical capability. Decades of further work likely required for anything approaching high-fidelity versions, if achievable.  
- **Partial elements**: Neural recording implants and basic sensory prostheses exist in limited medical use; external record/replay/display is routine; wireless data export is advancing.  
- **Closest real analogs**: Medical BCIs (https://neuralink.com/, https://synchron.com/), visual/auditory prostheses (NEI/NIDCD links), memory prosthesis research (DARPA RAM), and non-implant life-logging.
