**A. Analysis of proximity and remaining gaps**

The thesis describes whole-brain emulation / high-fidelity mind uploading: non-destructively or destructively scanning a human brain at sufficient resolution to capture the information that implements a person's mind, then running a functionally equivalent simulation that experiences subjective continuity (or at least behavioral and reportable continuity) and can be accelerated, paused, or otherwise manipulated, including for forced labor and subjective-time torture.

**Current technological distance**  
We are extremely far from this capability. No high-fidelity digital copy of any human mind exists, nor of any mammal. The closest empirical milestones are:

- Complete structural connectomes of tiny organisms (C. elegans ~300 neurons; partial Drosophila and larval zebrafish). These are static wiring diagrams, not dynamic, molecularly detailed, or behaviorally complete simulations that preserve identity-like properties.
- Functional imaging and recording (fMRI, multi-electrode arrays, Neuropixels, calcium imaging) that capture coarse activity patterns or thousands of neurons, not the full synaptic, neuromodulatory, glial, and intracellular state of ~86 billion neurons and ~10¹⁴–10¹⁵ synapses.
- Brain-computer interfaces (Neuralink, Synchron, academic Utah arrays, etc.) that read/write limited signals for motor or sensory prosthetics. These are low-bandwidth and do not extract a mind.
- Large-scale AI models that can imitate linguistic style or role-play personalities from text data. These are not substrate-independent copies of a specific biological mind; they lack the causal history, embodiment, and fine-grained cognitive architecture of the original person.

Expert surveys (e.g., from the Machine Intelligence Research Institute, Future of Humanity Institute historical estimates, and neuroscience roadmaps) place high-fidelity human whole-brain emulation decades to well over a century away under optimistic assumptions, or potentially never if key theoretical barriers prove insurmountable. Ray Kurzweil-style timelines are outliers and have repeatedly slipped.

**Major remaining gaps**

1. **Scanning / measurement**  
   Required: non-destructive or carefully destructive imaging at nanometer (or better) resolution across an entire living or freshly preserved human brain, capturing not only connectivity but synaptic weights, receptor types, neuromodulator distributions, glial states, epigenetic marks, and ongoing molecular dynamics. Current electron microscopy can do small volumes at high resolution but is slow, destructive, and incomplete for dynamics. In-vivo methods lack the resolution by many orders of magnitude. Preservation techniques (aldehyde-stabilized cryopreservation, etc.) are experimental and unproven for information-theoretic survival of identity.

2. **Understanding the neural code and necessary level of detail**  
   We do not know which physical details are computationally relevant to cognition, memory, and subjective experience. Synaptic connectomes alone are almost certainly insufficient; molecular and temporal dynamics matter. There is no validated theory that tells us the minimal sufficient emulation grain.

3. **Simulation substrate and software**  
   Even if a complete dataset existed, running a real-time or accelerated biophysically detailed simulation of a human brain exceeds present and near-term exascale computing by large factors when ion channels, diffusion, and plasticity are included. Abstraction layers that preserve function while reducing cost are unknown. Validation that the simulation is “the same mind” (vs. a philosophical zombie or divergent entity) has no accepted test.

4. **Subjective time control and “torture” primitives**  
   Once a runnable emulation existed, varying the clock speed of the simulation relative to wall-clock time is conceptually straightforward in software. Creating stable, reportable suffering, forced goal architectures, or obedience under threat of subjective eons would require solving alignment/control problems for digital minds that we have not solved even for far simpler AI systems. Instability, value drift, or shutdown resistance would be expected.

5. **Societal, legal, and economic gaps**  
   - No jurisdiction has a legal category for digital persons or copies; existing law treats brain data as medical/privacy information, not as a potentially sentient substrate.  
   - Consent, ownership, and rights of copies are philosophically contested and legally undefined. Non-consensual extraction would violate bodily integrity, data-protection, and (if the copy is sentient) anti-slavery and anti-torture norms.  
   - Economic incentives for “mind assistants” exist in science fiction and some AGI discourse, but the technical prerequisites are absent, so no serious industrial program is currently deploying this.  
   - Ethical and safety research (digital minds welfare, substrate-independent minds rights) is nascent and mostly academic.

**Bottom line on closeness**  
Society is not close. The thesis remains science fiction. Incremental progress in connectomics, neural recording density, and AI will continue, but the integrated capability—high-fidelity extraction + runnable conscious-equivalent simulation + reliable control for forced labor/torture—is blocked by multiple independent, deep scientific and engineering gaps. Timelines are highly uncertain; responsible estimates treat it as a long-term, high-uncertainty prospect rather than an imminent engineering project. Public and policy discussion is still largely speculative.
