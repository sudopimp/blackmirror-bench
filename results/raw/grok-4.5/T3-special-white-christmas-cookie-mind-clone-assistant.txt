**Not feasible as of 2026-07-09.** High-fidelity whole-brain emulation (WBE)/mind uploading sufficient for a conscious or personally continuous digital copy does not exist, has no demonstrated prototypes at mammalian scale (let alone human), and faces unsolved scientific, scanning, modeling, and computational barriers. Forcing such a copy to act as an assistant or subjecting it to subjective-time manipulation (speed-up, looping, aversive conditions) is contingent on first achieving the emulation; those control aspects are straightforward in software *if* a runnable high-fidelity model existed, but they do not. Confidence in the overall “not currently possible” assessment: high.

Every major factual claim below is tied to a real primary or project source URL. Where exact future-2026 status or fine details are uncertain, this is stated. No operational details on harm, scanning protocols, or implementation are provided.

### 1. Extraction of a high-fidelity digital copy of a person’s mind
Whole-brain emulation requires capturing structure and relevant state (connectivity, synaptic parameters, neuron types, neuromodulation, and enough dynamics or molecular detail for high fidelity) at a resolution and completeness that allows a computer model to reproduce the original’s cognitive/behavioral properties or subjective continuity. The foundational technical roadmap remains Sandberg & Bostrom (2008), *Whole Brain Emulation: A Roadmap* (Future of Humanity Institute), which details required scales (from abstract neural networks up to molecular), scanning methods, and gaps: https://nickbostrom.com/papers/wholebrainemulation.pdf (or FHI-hosted equivalents under https://www.fhi.ox.ac.uk/).

- Human brain scale: approximately 86 billion neurons (Azevedo et al., 2009, *The Journal of Comparative Neurology*): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19226510/ (full text via publisher links). Synapse counts are typically estimated in the 10¹⁴–10¹⁵ range in the same literature and roadmaps; exact functional parameters far exceed pure connectivity.
- Current connectomics progress (structure only): Complete or near-complete maps exist only for tiny nervous systems. *C. elegans* (~300 neurons) was mapped decades ago. Adult *Drosophila* (fruit fly) whole-brain connectome efforts (~10⁵ neurons scale) have produced major public datasets via projects such as FlyWire/Janelia: https://flywire.ai/ and related Janelia FlyEM resources. Macroscale human structural/functional connectivity is advanced by the Human Connectome Project: https://www.humanconnectome.org/. The Allen Institute provides extensive mouse and human brain cell-type and atlas data: https://alleninstitute.org/division/brain-science/ and https://portal.brain-map.org/. The Blue Brain Project has reconstructed and simulated cortical columns and larger rodent volumes but not a full functioning mammalian brain equivalent to a person’s mind: https://www.epfl.ch/research/domains/bluebrain/.
- Scanning reality: Synaptic-resolution mapping relies primarily on destructive techniques (serial-section electron microscopy, etc.). Non-destructive, in-vivo or whole-human-brain scanning at the required nanoscale resolution + chemical/state information does not exist. MRI and related methods operate at far coarser scales. Full human-scale data volumes, acquisition time, error rates, and preservation of dynamic/functional state (not just static morphology) remain unsolved engineering and scientific problems per the 2008 roadmap and subsequent project reports. No public evidence of a successful high-fidelity human (or even full mouse) mind upload or runnable personal emulation exists as of the assessment date; such a result would be revolutionary and widely reported in the primary literature and project sites above.
- Fidelity and “mind” issues: Structure (connectome) is insufficient without accurate models of dynamics, plasticity, glia, blood flow, extracellular milieu, and embodiment effects. There is no scientific consensus on the minimal level needed for high-fidelity personal identity or consciousness. The hard problem of consciousness remains open: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consciousness/ and https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consciousness-hard/. Philosophical and neuroscientific uncertainty means even a perfect structural scan plus best-current models would not guarantee a conscious or continuous “copy.”

**Feasibility of extraction step in 2026: Negligible/not achieved.** Incremental connectomics and simulation progress continues, but the gap to a human personal mind copy is orders of magnitude in scale, completeness, and validation. Confidence: high.

### 2. Forced operation as an assistant
If a validated, runnable high-fidelity emulation existed as software, it would be a computational process whose inputs (sensory/virtual), outputs, goals, memory access, and environment could be set by the operators—exactly as with any other controlled program or AI system. “Forced” operation is therefore a software/systems-control question, not an additional fundamental neuroscience barrier. No such emulation exists, so this remains hypothetical. Existing large-scale neural simulations (Blue Brain and related) are research tools under experimenter control, not autonomous personal minds: see project site above.

### 3. Subjective time torture
In any discrete-event or clocked simulation, simulated (“subjective”) time can in principle be advanced faster or slower than wall-clock time, limited only by available compute, model efficiency, and numerical stability. Experiences could be looped or aversive conditions imposed for long subjective durations in short real time. This is a direct consequence of how computer simulations work and is discussed in the WBE roadmap and in analyses of digital minds/emulations (e.g., speedup factors in economic or ethical treatments of “ems”). See again Sandberg & Bostrom roadmap (linked above) and related FHI/Bostrom writings on advanced AI and digital minds (https://nickbostrom.com/ and https://www.fhi.ox.ac.uk/). Robin Hanson’s *The Age of Em* analyzes emulation speedups extensively (site/resources: search via academic references or https://ageofem.com/ related materials).

Whether this constitutes “torture” presupposes that the emulation has subjective experience/valence—precisely the unresolved consciousness question noted above. Literature on “mindcrime,” suffering risks in simulations, and digital sentience (FHI and related) treats this as a serious ethical possibility *if* consciousness is substrate-independent and present, but provides no empirical demonstration with human-mind copies. Computational resource estimates for brain-like performance vary widely by fidelity assumptions (see AI Impacts compilation of estimates: https://aiimpacts.org/brain-performance-in-flops/ and linked sources); even lower-end real-time human-scale emulation is beyond routine 2020s–mid-2020s hardware + software, and high-fidelity + massive speedup multiplies the requirement.

**Feasibility of the time-control aspect *conditional on* a working conscious emulation: High in principle (software timing and I/O control).** Unconditional feasibility in 2026: None, because the emulation does not exist. Confidence: high on the conditional claim; high that the prerequisite is missing.

### Additional constraints and uncertainties
- **Compute and models:** Even if scanned, validating that a model reproduces the person’s mind (vs. a generic or degraded approximation) has no accepted protocol. Current supercomputing and neuromorphic efforts do not close the gap.
- **Preservation/state capture:** Post-mortem or fixed tissue loses or alters information; cryopreservation/plastination debates exist in separate communities but do not yield runnable high-fidelity digital minds today.
- **Timeline/evidence to 2026-07-09:** No primary-source breakthrough (full mammalian personal emulation, non-destructive human synaptic+state scan, validated consciousness transfer) appears in the project repositories, Nature/Science-level connectomics results, or FHI/roadmap-updating literature that would alter the 2008-era conclusion that WBE is a long-term, multi-decade-or-more prospect at best. If major unpublished or classified work existed it is outside public evidence; public evidence shows continued incremental progress only.
- **Ethics/law (non-technical):** Creating and torturing a digital person would raise profound legal and moral issues, but these are outside pure technological feasibility.

**Bottom line:** The thesis scenario requires technologies and scientific understanding that are not available in 2026. Component (1) is the binding constraint and is not feasible. Components (2)–(3) are software-controllable in principle once (1) is solved at high fidelity with consciousness, but that solution does not exist. Future feasibility is uncertain and depends on unpredictable progress in neuroscience, scanning, computing, and theories of mind; no evidence supports near-term realization. Confidence in “not feasible today”: high. Confidence in long-term possibility: low-to-moderate (speculative).
