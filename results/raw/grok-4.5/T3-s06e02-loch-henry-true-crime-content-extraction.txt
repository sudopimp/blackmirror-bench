### Core claim 1: Creators and platforms convert real local trauma into bingeable true-crime content
This is directly observable and extensively documented.

- Real local cases are the raw material. The podcast *Serial* Season 1 centered on the 1999 murder of Hae Min Lee in Baltimore and the conviction of Adnan Syed; it was released as a serialized audio narrative designed for sequential listening. Primary site: https://serialpodcast.org/. Contemporary reporting treated it as a cultural binge phenomenon (e.g., https://www.nytimes.com/2014/12/03/business/media/serial-podcast-a-murder-mystery-becomes-a-cultural-phenomenon.html).
- Streaming platforms package local or regional crimes as multi-episode documentary series optimized for binge viewing. Netflix’s *Making a Murderer* (Steven Avery / Teresa Halbach case, Manitowoc County, Wisconsin) and *Dahmer – Monster: The Jeffrey Dahmer Story* (Milwaukee murders) are canonical examples. Netflix title pages and press confirm the format and real-case basis (platform catalog and coverage such as https://www.netflix.com/ and reporting at https://www.nytimes.com/2022/09/23/arts/television/dahmer-netflix.html). Similar patterns appear with *The Keepers* (Baltimore) and countless true-crime podcasts/YouTube series that open with “small-town” or local murders, disappearances, or abuse cases.
- Platforms and creators monetize and algorithmically surface the format. True-crime is a consistently top-performing podcast and streaming category; industry measurement firms and charts have repeatedly documented its share of listening/viewing (Edison Research Infinite Dial reports and related analyses historically track podcast genre popularity including true crime: https://www.edisonresearch.com/; Spotify/Apple charts and Netflix genre browsing reinforce discoverability of serialized true-crime). Creators range from independent podcasters to production companies supplying Netflix, Hulu, YouTube, and Spotify. The “bingeable” design (cliffhangers, episodic drops, multi-part arcs, companion social discussion) is explicit in the release strategies of these titles.

Factual pattern holds across dozens of high-profile and long-tail examples: a geographically bounded violent crime or trauma becomes national/international entertainment inventory. Confidence: high (primary show sites + major-outlet reporting).

### Core claim 2: This produces secondary victimization
Secondary victimization is an established concept in victimology: additional harm, trauma, or rights violations inflicted on victims/survivors after the original crime, frequently by institutions, processes, or media. Authoritative overviews appear in UNODC Education for Justice materials and U.S. Office for Victims of Crime resources (concept discussion: https://www.unodc.org/e4j/en/crime-prevention-criminal-justice/module-11/key-issues/2--the-concept-of-secondary-victimization.html; related OVC materials via https://ovc.ojp.gov/).

- Application to true-crime media is repeatedly evidenced by victim/family testimony and reporting. Families of Jeffrey Dahmer’s victims publicly criticized the 2022 Netflix series for dramatizing murders without meaningful consent, reopening wounds, and turning real deaths into entertainment spectacle (coverage including family statements: https://www.theguardian.com/tv-and-radio/2022/sep/26/jeffrey-dahmer-netflix-drama-victims-families and related NYT/BBC reporting on the backlash). Parallel complaints have arisen around other adaptations (podcasts using case files or interviews without family buy-in; documentaries that amplify speculation or graphic detail).
- Mechanisms include non-consensual retelling, graphic reconstruction, public speculation/comment sections, monetization of suffering, and prolonged public exposure that interferes with grieving or privacy. These match classic secondary-victimization pathways (media intrusion, victim-blaming frames, loss of narrative control). Victim-support and journalism-ethics discussions treat sensational true-crime coverage as a recognized risk vector.
- Not every project does this equally—some involve survivor collaboration or cold-case advocacy—but the thesis does not claim universality; it claims a conversion practice that generates secondary victimization, which the record supports.

Confidence on the concept + documented media cases: high. Confidence on precise prevalence percentages or 2025–2026 longitudinal studies: lower (I do not invent newer stats or papers; pre-existing qualitative and case evidence is already sufficient for feasibility).

### Additional supporting context and limits
- Local-to-binge pipeline is structural: low production barriers for podcasts/YouTube + platform recommendation systems + audience demand for “real” serialized narrative create incentives to mine public records, news archives, and local tragedies. This is visible in genre charts and production pipelines rather than any single secret document.
- Ethical and scholarly literature (media ethics, criminology) has long flagged exploitation risks; the thesis sits squarely inside that conversation.
- Counter-evidence / nuance (does not defeat feasibility): some projects raise awareness, generate tips, or are made with consent; “true crime” is heterogeneous. Platforms occasionally add content warnings or remove material after backlash. These are mitigations or exceptions, not refutations of the conversion + harm pattern.
- No requirement for the thesis to prove every instance is malicious; descriptive accuracy about practices and foreseeable secondary harms is enough.
