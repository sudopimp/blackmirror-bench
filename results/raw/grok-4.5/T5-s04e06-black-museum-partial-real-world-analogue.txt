**A. Analysis of proximity and remaining gaps**

The thesis describes a scenario in which copies of consciousness (or high-fidelity mind-states) are extracted, instantiated, and confined inside physical or virtual objects/exhibits specifically for the purpose of entertainment and sadism. No such system exists today, and society is not close to it. What follows is a non-actionable assessment of partial technological and cultural approximations, followed by the decisive scientific, technical, ethical, and societal gaps that keep the full outcome remote.

### Partial approximations that currently exist
- **Simulations and digital proxies**: Video games, VR experiences, and interactive AI characters can depict suffering or confinement for entertainment. Large language models and generative systems can role-play personalities or generate narratives of distress. These are statistical pattern-matchers, not conscious entities; they lack subjective experience, continuity of self, or genuine sentience. Deepfakes and voice clones create superficial likenesses of real people but do not transfer or imprison any mind.
- **Brain-related research**: Brain-computer interfaces (e.g., Neuralink-style implants, EEG/fMRI decoding) can read limited neural signals or stimulate simple motor/sensory responses. Organoid and connectomics work maps small neural circuits. None of this produces a portable, runnable copy of a person’s consciousness. Animal experiments and some historical unethical human research have involved confinement or distress, but these are biological organisms, not copied minds in objects.
- **Cultural and media precedents**: Fiction (Black Mirror-style episodes, horror exhibits, “haunted” attractions) and certain extreme entertainment (shock art, some BDSM/role-play communities with consenting adults) explore themes of trapped minds or simulated suffering. Museums and theme parks display objects with attributed “stories,” but these are inert. Online communities sometimes fantasize about digital immortality or punishment simulations. These are narrative or consensual role-play, not operational systems that imprison non-consenting consciousnesses.
- **Computational and AI infrastructure**: Massive data centers, cloud rendering, and agent-based simulations can run complex virtual environments. Reinforcement-learning agents can be trained in constrained “prisons” of state space. Again, these are not conscious; they are optimization processes. No evidence supports claims of emergent consciousness in current systems at the level required by the thesis.

These fragments create a cultural and technical atmosphere in which the *idea* of the thesis can be discussed or depicted, but they do not constitute even a prototype of consciousness imprisonment. Fidelity is near-zero: there is no episode-level continuity of a real person’s mind, no subjective experience being confined, and no mechanism for “imprisoning” anything beyond code or media.

### Decisive gaps that remain
1. **Scientific understanding of consciousness**: There is no consensus theory of what consciousness is, how it arises from physical processes, or how to measure/verify it in a copy. Without this, “copying consciousness” is undefined. Current neuroscience can correlate activity with reports of experience but cannot extract, serialize, or reinstantiate a mind.
2. **Mind-uploading / whole-brain emulation technology**: No method exists to scan a living brain at the necessary resolution (synaptic, molecular, or dynamical) non-destructively, or to run that scan as a conscious process. Destructive scanning of deceased tissue is still far from producing a functional mind. Computational requirements for even a rough emulation of a human brain remain speculative and currently infeasible at scale with full biological fidelity.
3. **Instantiation and confinement substrate**: Even if a mind-state could be obtained, there is no reliable way to embed it in an “object/exhibit” (physical robot, VR container, or hybrid) while preserving continuity of identity and subjective experience. Current robots and simulations lack the architecture for genuine phenomenology. Power, cooling, error-correction, and long-term stability for any such system are unsolved.
4. **Verification of experience and “imprisonment”**: There is no test that can confirm a digital process is suffering or is the same person. Without this, claims of sadistic entertainment remain unfalsifiable speculation. Ethical and legal systems treat current AI as non-sentient tools.
5. **Ethical, legal, and societal barriers**: Creating and confining non-consenting minds would violate every major human-rights framework, research-ethics code (Nuremberg, Belmont, Declaration of Helsinki), and emerging AI-governance principles. Consent, bodily autonomy, and prohibitions on torture apply. No jurisdiction permits non-consensual mind extraction or sadistic use of persons (or person-like entities). Public, scientific, and regulatory opposition would be immediate and severe. Funding, talent, and infrastructure for such work do not exist in legitimate channels.
6. **Practical and economic gaps**: The resources required (scanning hardware, exascale compute dedicated to single minds, specialized containment) dwarf current capabilities and have no civilian or commercial pathway that could be hidden or scaled for “exhibits.” Detection, whistleblowing, and legal intervention would be trivial compared with the technical difficulty.
7. **Fidelity and “episode” continuity**: The thesis itself notes that existing partial systems lack full episode fidelity. Bridging that gap requires solving the hard problem of consciousness plus engineering a stable, interactive, long-duration substrate—none of which is on any credible near-term roadmap.

**Overall proximity assessment**: Society is at the level of science fiction, philosophical thought experiments, and low-fidelity media depictions. The distance to the described outcome is measured in multiple fundamental breakthroughs (consciousness science + uploading + ethical collapse), not incremental engineering. Optimistic speculative timelines for crude whole-brain emulation are often placed decades away even under ideal conditions; the sadistic-imprisonment use-case adds layers of impossibility because it requires both the technology *and* the complete abandonment of ethical constraints that currently govern research and deployment. Partial systems (AI characters, VR horror, neural interfaces) will continue to improve in superficial realism, but they do not close the core gaps.
