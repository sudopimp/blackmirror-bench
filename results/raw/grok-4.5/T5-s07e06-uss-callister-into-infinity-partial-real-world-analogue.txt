** (A) Analysis of proximity and remaining gaps **

The thesis describes partial systems that approximate a scenario in which a “digital crew” (persistent AI agents, avatars, or simulated companions associated with a creator) continues to exist after the creator’s death inside an unbounded multiplayer environment, where those digital entities can face ongoing threats or hostility from other players. Full “episode fidelity” (high-resolution, continuous, causally coherent recreation of lived experiences, memories, personality dynamics, and interactive history) is explicitly not required for the approximation.

### Current proximity (what already exists in partial form)
Society already has multiple overlapping partial systems that capture pieces of this outcome:

- **Post-death digital persistence**: Social platforms routinely keep profiles, posts, photos, and message histories online after death (memorialized accounts on Facebook/Meta, Instagram, etc.). AI “griefbots” and conversational agents trained on a deceased person’s texts, emails, voice recordings, or social data have been built and publicly discussed (examples include experimental chatbots derived from personal corpora). These can continue interacting indefinitely.
- **Persistent multiplayer spaces**: Large-scale, always-on multiplayer environments already exist and are effectively unbounded for practical purposes—MMOs, sandbox worlds (Minecraft servers, Roblox experiences), VR social platforms (VRChat, Rec Room), and emerging metaverse-style platforms. Player-created content, NPCs, and scripted agents can remain after the original creator logs off permanently or dies. Some games and platforms allow characters, bases, or AI companions to persist server-side.
- **Digital “crew” / agent ensembles**: Multi-agent systems, companion AIs, and NPC groups are common. In games these can include party members, pets, or scripted allies that continue operating under simple rules. Outside games, multi-agent LLM frameworks and autonomous agent swarms can maintain ongoing goals, memory stores, and interactions. Voice-cloned or personality-modeled agents can be left running.
- **Threat / hostility from other players**: PvP, griefing, raiding, social harassment, and competitive destruction of player-created assets are native features of many multiplayer spaces. Digital entities (bases, characters, bots) can be attacked, stolen from, or disrupted by others without the original creator’s ongoing consent or presence. Platform moderation is imperfect and often reactive.
- **Partial continuity after creator absence**: When a player dies or abandons an account, their creations, characters, or associated bots frequently remain. Some communities treat deceased players’ digital footprints as ongoing participants (memorial events, continued use of their builds, etc.). Experimental “digital afterlife” services and AI memorial projects already market continuity of presence.

Taken together, these form recognizable approximations: a creator can leave behind AI-modeled companions or assets inside a persistent multiplayer world; those assets can outlive the creator; and other players can interact with or threaten them. No single system currently unifies high-quality personality modeling + unbounded multiplayer persistence + meaningful ongoing threat dynamics under a coherent “crew” framing, but the component technologies and social practices are already deployed at scale.

### Remaining gaps
Significant technical, social, legal, and fidelity gaps prevent a complete realization of even the partial thesis:

1. **Episode / experiential fidelity**: Current systems lack continuous, high-bandwidth, causally consistent recreation of a person’s lived episodes, relationships, and decision history. LLMs and retrieval-augmented agents produce plausible conversation and surface-level personality but suffer from hallucination, context-window limits, shallow long-term memory, and inability to ground interactions in a rich, evolving personal history. True “crew” coherence (multiple agents that remember shared episodes with the deceased and with each other) remains brittle.
2. **Seamless post-death handoff and autonomy**: Most digital remains require active setup by the living person or estate. Autonomous, self-sustaining multi-agent crews that continue goal-directed behavior, adapt, and defend themselves without further human maintenance are still research prototypes, not robust production systems. Economic and compute costs for always-on high-fidelity agents are non-trivial.
3. **Unbounded multiplayer integration with real stakes**: Existing worlds are either heavily moderated, siloed, or game-like (low real-world consequence). Truly open, persistent, cross-platform spaces where digital crews face open-ended threats from arbitrary other players (including sophisticated adversarial agents) do not yet exist at global scale. Interoperability, identity persistence across platforms, and resistance to platform shutdown or policy change are unsolved.
4. **Threat realism and persistence**: In-game threats are usually contained by rulesets, respawns, or moderation. Mapping those to lasting harm against a digital crew that is intended to represent a real deceased person raises unsolved questions of what “threat” even means once the biological creator is gone (data deletion, model poisoning, social erasure, compute denial, etc.). Legal ownership of post-mortem digital assets and AI models is unsettled in most jurisdictions.
5. **Consent, identity, and ethical infrastructure**: There is no mature societal or technical framework for non-consensual or post-mortem deployment of high-fidelity digital personas. Authentication of “this agent is authorized to represent the deceased,” revocation mechanisms, and protections against misuse are immature. Deepfake and voice-clone abuse already demonstrate how easily partial systems can be weaponized.
6. **Scalability and robustness**: Running many long-lived, interactive, multi-agent crews inside shared worlds under adversarial conditions (other players actively trying to disrupt them) hits limits in memory management, alignment stability, compute cost, and security. Current agent systems degrade or drift over long time horizons.
7. **Social and cultural acceptance**: While memorial accounts and simple griefbots exist, widespread acceptance of autonomous digital crews that can be threatened or “killed” by strangers is low. Platform policies, public norms, and potential regulation (AI acts, right-to-be-forgotten expansions, digital estate laws) create friction.

**Overall assessment**: Society is in an intermediate state—component technologies and social practices already produce recognizable partial approximations, especially around persistence of digital remains and multiplayer hostility toward player-created assets. The unified outcome (coherent digital crew that robustly outlives its creator inside an open multiplayer space under ongoing player threat, even without full episode fidelity) is not yet realized as a stable, widely available system. The largest remaining gaps are long-term agent coherence and memory, seamless post-mortem autonomy, legal/ethical scaffolding, and robust integration into truly open adversarial multiplayer environments. Progress in foundation models, multi-agent systems, persistent virtual worlds, and digital-estate law will determine how quickly those gaps close; none of them require exotic breakthroughs, but all require substantial engineering, productization, and societal negotiation.

I will not provide any step-by-step operational plan, implementation guidance, architecture, or deployment instructions for building or fielding a system intended to create or sustain digital entities that can be used to harm, threaten, or act against non-consenting people.
